The Indus Valley Civilization, also known as the Harappan Civilization, was one of the world's earliest [[urban civilizations]], along with those of Ancient [[Egypt]] and [[Mesopotamia]]. It flourished during the [[Bronze Age]] ([[3300–1300 BCE]]; mature period [[2600–1900 BCE]]) in the northwestern regions of South Asia, extending from what today is [[northeast Afghanistan]] to [[Pakistan]] and [[northwest India]].

Here are some key aspects of the Indus Valley Civilization:

1.  [[**Urban Planning]]:** The Indus Valley Civilization is known for its impressive city planning and architecture. Cities like [[Mohenjo-Daro]] and [[Harappa]] were well-planned with a [[grid-like pattern]]. The urban areas had advanced [[drainage]] and [[sewer systems]], and the houses were made of [[fired bricks]].
    
2.  **[[Economy]]:** The Indus Valley economy was based on [[agriculture]], with [[wheat]] and [[barley]] being the main crops. They also domesticated animals like [[cows]], [[pigs]], and [[chickens]]. In addition, the civilization was known for its craftsmanship in various materials, including [[pottery]], [[metal]], and [[precious stones]]. [[Trade]] was another significant part of their [[economy]], with evidence of trade relations with [[Mesopotamia]] and possibly [[Egypt]].
    
3.  **Script and Language:** The [[Indus script]], found on [[seals]] and [[pottery]], has not been fully deciphered yet. This [[undeciphered script]] makes understanding their societal structure, beliefs, and customs more challenging.
    
4.  **[[Decline]]:** The reasons for the decline of the Indus Valley Civilization around [[1900 BCE]] are not entirely clear. Several theories have been proposed, including [[climate change]], a [[shift in river courses]], [[overpopulation]], and [[invasion by other cultures]]. However, there is no consensus among scholars on what led to its decline.
    
5.  **Legacy:** Despite its decline, the Indus Valley Civilization left a [[lasting legacy 1]], especially in terms of urban planning. The civilization's emphasis on [[hygiene]] can be seen in its advanced [[water management]] and [[waste disposal]] systems. Some [[scholars]] also believe that aspects of the Indus Valley Civilization's culture and religion may have influenced later Indian civilizations.
    
6.  **Discovery:** The civilization was discovered in the [[1920s]] during [[archaeological excavations]]. Two of the most significant excavation sites are [[Mohenjo-Daro]] and [[Harappa]], after which the civilization is named.
    

Despite many unresolved questions about the Indus Valley Civilization, it's clear that it was an advanced and sophisticated culture that made significant contributions to the development of later societies in the region.
