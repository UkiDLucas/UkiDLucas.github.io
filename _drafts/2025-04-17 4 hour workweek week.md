Rereading The[[ 4-Hour Workweek]] in 2025, I’m seeing it with fresh eyes—and applying it with 2025 tools. Tim Ferriss was way ahead of his time in advocating for location independence, time freedom, and the automation of income. But the tools and mindset shifts needed to implement his principles have evolved. Here’s what I’m doing now:

  

  

  

1. Replace “deferred life plan” with “today life design”

Instead of waiting until retirement, I’ve broken my year into mini-retirements—some weeks in Greece, mountain running in the PNW, and working from the farm in Michigan. Life isn’t about escaping work; it’s about integrating work that excites me with time that rejuvenates me.

  

2. 80/20 every area of life

Ferriss hammered on the Pareto Principle. I’ve started applying it to everything:

  

- Which 20% of workouts give me 80% of my fitness?
- Which 20% of clients give me 80% of my income?
- Which 20% of my wardrobe do I wear 80% of the time?  
    This led me to fire some clients, automate email filters, and only wear black t-shirts and Wranglers.

  

  

3. Email is now AI-filtered

Back then, Ferriss recommended checking email twice a day. Now I have a local LLM scan and summarize important action items from Gmail and Outlook into a daily Obsidian note. AI triages everything. I only step in to reply personally when it truly matters.

  

4. Muse business = micro SaaS

Instead of the “dropshipping vitamin company” model of 2007, I’m building tiny AI-powered tools that solve very specific problems (e.g., daily farm task planner, Obsidian plugin, language-learning companions). I charge a few bucks a month. It adds up. I call it “micro wealth.”

  

5. Delegation: from VA to AI

In 2009, Ferriss said to hire a virtual assistant. In 2025, I train GPT-powered agents to handle scheduling, writing, and decision-making within bounds I define. For more creative work, I still use humans—but the repetitive stuff? Automated.

  

6. Time = sacred, not just blocked

Ferriss talked about blocking off time for your priorities. I’ve taken it further: I don’t take meetings before 10am, and I never take meetings on Wednesdays. Morning runs, deep work, and journaling come first. I protect them like my life depends on it—because it does.

  

7. Mini-retirements = project sprints

Instead of checking out for a month, I now sprint for 2–4 weeks on a single project, then rest and reset. It’s seasonal, just like my farm. Work is a rhythm, not a grind.

  

  

  

Two key lessons I’ll blog about:

  

- “Build your AI army before burnout hits.”  
    Waiting to automate your life until you’re drowning is a rookie move. Start with one thing you hate. Train your bot. Liberate an hour. Repeat.
- “Don’t chase freedom. Design it.”  
    Freedom doesn’t appear when you quit your job or hit $10k/month. It starts the moment you write down what your ideal Tuesday looks like—and build towards it. Slowly. Quietly. Every day.

  

  

As Ferriss wrote, “Life doesn’t have to be so damn hard.” In 2025, we have better tools than ever to make that true.
