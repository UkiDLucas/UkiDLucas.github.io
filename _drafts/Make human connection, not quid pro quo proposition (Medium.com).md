To make a "quid pro quo" proposition, from Latin, "something for something" means that you reach out to someone assuming that the other party needs something from you, for example, the business you might want to offer. 
The statement has gained a negative meaning, but in the context of this article I use it in its original meaning, it means to barter. A win-win exchange. 

# Motivation

When I was living in Chicago, I was running a Google Technology Developers Community, I organized and often presented at, over a hundred conferences and developed a relatively large network of over 13,000 LinkedIn contacts. 

I love having all these connections, but it comes with a burden, too, a lot of people are reaching out with "cold" offers. 

I would like to interact with my social network in a way that is much more pleasant and beneficial to all. 

# Cold contacts

People initializing cold contacts usually are hungry for quick success. 

They think that if they quickly get to the point they will appear professional, and efficient, and will not waste each other time.

I appreciate brevity. People who work with me know that a phrase I use in the meetings very often is "OK, let's move on".

Yet, this "cold" approach in email communication, and especially via Social Networks like LinkedIn, Meetup, etc., in my opinion, is totally wrong.

# What do we really care about?

I have quite a few people that I am happy to chat with. 

Here are my five characteristics of positive professional relationships:

1.  **Seeing Beyond Immediate Goals:** It's essential to approach others as individuals, not as opportunities. Seeing beyond the immediate gains will foster a connection that leaves a lasting impression.
    
2.  **Sharing Personal Interests, and Aspirations:** By opening up about our lives, we invite a similar openness from others, paving the way for mutual connection and understanding.
    
3.  **Asking the Right Questions:** Genuine curiosity makes us stand out. It shows we care about the person, not just the potential benefits. It gives the other person the pleasure of becoming a mentor. This is important as we all wish others will find us interesting and useful.
    
4.  **Building Mutual Trust and Understanding:** Trust can't be rushed. It's cultivated over time and creates the foundation for deeper connections.
    
5.  **Respecting Each Other's Time:** Our time is precious, as is that of others. Demonstrating respect for each other's time underscores our sincerity.



## Building trust relationships

Trust comes with profound rewards. It's through the gradual development of the relationship, and trust, that our cold messages transform from white noise to welcomed communication with a trusted person.


Once the relationship and the trust are established, it is natural to ask how can I help you, what is that company you are working for, and so on. 


## Conclusion

In the end, successful networking is more about the quality of our connections than a quid pro quo exchange. While cold contacts may seem efficient, the time and effort spent in building genuine relationships yield far greater results. Our messages become not just another email to be ignored, but a communication from a respected and trusted contact. It's in this space of trust and connection that our networking truly blossoms.
