How do I learn the British English accent?

](https://www.quora.com/How-do-I-learn-the-British-English-accent?no_redirect=1)

There are various kinds of accent in Britain. For RP (Received Pronunciation), I suggest you take the following steps:  

1. Don't be afraid to bother to learn the IPA (International Phonetic Alphabet) because it really tells you the differences between RP and GA (General American). Learn the vowels and consonants one by one.
2. Compare and grasp the differences between corresponding vowels in RP and GA. For example, the British "low" and the American "low" are quite different. However, some differences are too subtle that they cannot be embodied in IPA, in which case you need to use your ear to distinguish, such as the British "apple" and the American "apple". For some words, the vowels are totally different, in which case you need to understand under what circumstances they are different. For example, in word such as grasp, half and bath, where the letter a appears before fricative consonants, the RP for the letter a is [ɑː] instead of [[æ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-open_front_unrounded_vowel "en.wikipedia.org")].
3. There are a few differences in consonants between RP and GA. In RP, r is only pronunced before vowels, and t-glottalization is less common. When l appears between two vowels, such as "belly" it is only prononced as one hard l in RP, but a soft l followed by a hard l in GA.
4. After mastering how to pronounce words, you need to learn how to speak in an RP tone. I think this is the most difficult and tricky part of RP. You can compare the differences in the same sentence type or structure between RP and GA.

For all the steps mentioned above, you can find a lot of examples in BBC documents and news, British movies and TV plays. It can't be better if you have chances to talk directly to someone with an RP accent and let him/her help you with your accent.


1. Prosody. This is an often overlooked feature of language. This is the "song" of the language: its rise and falls. It's not just at the level of the word but also at the level of the phrase and further. Listen to native English speakers without concentrating on the words or meaning (Pretend you don't know English). Just listen to the rhythm of their speech. Try to emulate this cadence when you speak. English, like most languages, have a very distinct rhythm. If you speak English using your native tongue's rhythm, which you likely do, you make it difficult for listeners who are native English speakers to understand what you're saying. You tax the part of our brain that expects "normal" English rhythm. I wrote a [brief paper](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/261022308_BETTER_SECOND_LANGUAGE_ACQUISITION_BY_CONTINUOUS_IDENTIFICATION_OF_PROSODIC_FEATURES_IN_TEXT "www.researchgate.net") on this topic last year if you are interested. Look at page 17 in particular, which shows a method I was developing to help language learners visualize the rhythm of speech. (It's free to access.)

2. Vowels. Learn where in your mouth English vowels are pronounced. It's likely you've undertaken what linguists call fossilization. Basically, you've become proficient enough in English that you've stopped modifying your pronunciation. But if you've done that before learning exactly how to accurately produce English vowels, you'll keep a thick accent. And this can be a difficult task. You may find YouTube videos describing translating vowels from your native tongue to English. Vowels may be ever so slightly different.
