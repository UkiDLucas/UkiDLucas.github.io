#public 
#biology 

_**Camellia sinensis**_ is a [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") of evergreen [shrub](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shrub "Shrub") or small [tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree "Tree") in the [flowering plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flowering_plant "Flowering plant") family [Theaceae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaceae "Theaceae"). Its leaves and leaf buds are used to produce the popular beverage [tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea "Tea"). Common names include **tea plant**, **tea shrub**, and **tea tree** (unrelated to _[Melaleuca alternifolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melaleuca_alternifolia "Melaleuca alternifolia")_, the source of [tea tree oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea_tree_oil "Tea tree oil"), or the genus _[Leptospermum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leptospermum "Leptospermum")_ commonly called tea tree).

[White tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_tea "White tea"), [yellow tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_tea "Yellow tea"), [green tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_tea "Green tea"), [oolong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oolong "Oolong"), [dark tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_tea "Dark tea") (which includes [pu-erh tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pu-erh_tea "Pu-erh tea")) and [black tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_tea "Black tea") are all harvested from one of two major varieties grown today, _C. sinensis_ var. _sinensis_ and _C. s._ var. _assamica_,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-3) but are [processed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea_processing "Tea processing") differently to attain varying levels of [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") with black tea being the most oxidized and green being the least.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-4) [Kukicha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kukicha "Kukicha") ([twig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twig "Twig") tea) is also harvested from _C. sinensis_, but uses twigs and stems rather than leaves.

## Nomenclature and taxonomy[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Nomenclature and taxonomy")]

The [generic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus "Genus") name _Camellia_ is taken from the [Latinized name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latinized_name "Latinized name") of Rev. [Georg Kamel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Joseph_Kamel "Georg Joseph Kamel"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-5) [SJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society_of_Jesus "Society of Jesus") (1661–1706), a [Moravian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moravia "Moravia")-born [Jesuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesuit "Jesuit") lay brother, pharmacist, and missionary to the [Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines "Philippines").

[Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus") chose his name in 1753 for the genus to honor Kamel's contributions to botany[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-6) (although Kamel did not discover or name this plant, or any _Camellia_,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-7) and Linnaeus did not consider this plant a _Camellia_ but a _Thea_).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-8)

[Robert Sweet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Sweet_(botanist) "Robert Sweet (botanist)") shifted all formerly _Thea_ species to the genus _Camellia_ in 1818.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-9) The name _sinensis_ means "from China" in [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin").

Four varieties of _C. sinensis_ are recognized.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-FOC-2) Of these, _C. sinensis_ var. _sinensis_ and _C. s._ var. _assamica_ (JW Masters) Kitamura are most commonly used for tea, and _C. s._ var. _pubilimba_ Hung T. Chang and _C. s._ var. _dehungensis_ (Hung T. Chang & BH Chen) TL Ming are sometimes used locally.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-FOC-2) The Cambodia type tea (_C. assamica_ subsp. _lasiocaly_) was originally considered a type of assam tea. However, later genetic work showed that it is a hybrid between Chinese small leaf tea and assam type tea.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-10)

Tea plants are native to East Asia, and probably originated in the borderlands of north Burma and southwestern China.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Yamamoto-11)

- Chinese (small leaf) tea [_C. sinensis_ var. _sinensis_]
- Chinese Western Yunnan Assam (large leaf) tea [_C. sinensis_ var. _assamica_]
- Indian Assam (large leaf) tea [_C. sinensis_ var. _assamica_]
- Chinese Southern Yunnan Assam (large leaf) tea [_C. sinensis_ var. _assamica_]

Chinese (small leaf) tea may have originated in southern China possibly with hybridization of unknown wild tea relatives. However, since no wild populations of this tea are known, the precise location of its origin is speculative.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Meegahakumbura_1-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Meegahakumbura_2-13)

Given their genetic differences forming distinct [clades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clade "Clade"), Chinese Assam type tea (_C. s._ var. _assamica_) may have two different parentages – one being found in southern [Yunnan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan") ([Xishuangbanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xishuangbanna "Xishuangbanna"), [Pu'er City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pu%27er_City "Pu'er City")) and the other in western Yunnan ([Lincang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincang "Lincang"), [Baoshan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baoshan,_Yunnan "Baoshan, Yunnan")). Many types of Southern Yunnan Assam tea have been hybridized with the closely related species _[Camellia taliensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_taliensis "Camellia taliensis")._ Unlike Southern Yunnan Assam tea, Western Yunnan Assam tea shares many genetic similarities with Indian Assam type tea (also _C. s._ var. _assamica_). Thus, Western Yunnan Assam tea and Indian Assam tea both may have originated from the same parent plant in the area where southwestern China, Indo-Burma, and Tibet meet. However, as the Indian Assam tea shares no [haplotypes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haplotype "Haplotype") with Western Yunnan Assam tea, Indian Assam tea is likely to have originated from an independent domestication. Some Indian Assam tea appears to have hybridized with the species _[Camellia pubicosta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_pubicosta "Camellia pubicosta")._[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Meegahakumbura_1-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Meegahakumbura_2-13)

Assuming a generation of 12 years, Chinese small leaf tea is estimated to have diverged from Assam tea around 22,000 years ago, while Chinese Assam tea and Indian Assam tea diverged 2,800 years ago. This divergence tea would correspond to the last [glacial maximum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacial_maximum "Glacial maximum").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Meegahakumbura_1-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-Meegahakumbura_2-13)

Chinese small leaf type tea was introduced into India in 1836 by the British and some Indian Assam type tea (e.g. [Darjeeling tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darjeeling_tea "Darjeeling tea")) appear to be genetic hybrids of Chinese small leaf type tea, native Indian Assam, and possibly also closely related wild tea species.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-14)

## Description[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Description")]

_Camellia sinensis_ is native to [East Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia"), the [Indian Subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent "Indian Subcontinent"), and [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia"), but it is today cultivated all around the world in tropical and subtropical regions. It is an evergreen [shrub](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shrub "Shrub") or small [tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree "Tree") that is usually trimmed to below 2 m (6.6 ft) when cultivated for its leaves. It has a strong [taproot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taproot "Taproot"). The flowers are yellow-white, 2.5–4 cm (0.98–1.57 in) in diameter, with seven or eight petals.

[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Flower_of_Tea_plant.jpg "Enlarge")

The seeds of _C. sinensis_ and _[C. oleifera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_oleifera "Camellia oleifera")_ can be pressed to yield [tea oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea_oil "Tea oil"), a sweetish seasoning and cooking oil that should not be confused with [tea tree oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea_tree_oil "Tea tree oil"), an [essential oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_oil "Essential oil") that is used for medical and cosmetic purposes, and originates from the leaves of a different plant.

The leaves are 4–15 cm (1.6–5.9 in) long and 2–5 cm (0.79–1.97 in) broad. Fresh leaves contain about 4% [caffeine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caffeine "Caffeine"), as well as related compounds including [theobromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theobromine "Theobromine").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-18) The young, light-green leaves are preferably harvested for tea production; they have short, white hairs on the underside. Older leaves are deeper green. Different leaf ages produce differing tea qualities, since their chemical compositions are different. Usually, the tip (bud) and the first two to three leaves are harvested for processing. This hand picking is repeated every one to two weeks.

In 2017, Chinese scientists sequenced the genome of _C. s. var. assamica._[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-19) It contains about three billion base pairs, which was larger than most plants previously sequenced.[[GPUs with CuPy in Python]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-20)

## Cultivation[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Cultivation")]

Main article: [Tea cultivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea#Cultivation_and_harvesting "Tea")

_Camellia sinensis_ is mainly cultivated in tropical and subtropical climates, in areas with at least 127 cm (50 in) of rainfall a year. Tea plants prefer a rich and moist growing location in full to part sun, and can be grown in [hardiness zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardiness_zone "Hardiness zone") 7 – 9. However, the clonal one is commercially cultivated from the equator to as far north as [Cornwall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornwall "Cornwall") and [Scotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotland "Scotland") on the UK mainland.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-22) Many high quality teas are grown at high elevations, up to 2,200 m (7,200 ft), as the plants grow more slowly and acquire more flavor.

Tea plants will grow into a tree if left undisturbed, but cultivated plants are pruned to waist height for ease of plucking. Two principal varieties are used, the small-leaved Chinese variety plant (_C. s. sinensis_) and the large-leaved Assamese plant (_C. s. assamica_), used mainly for black tea.

### Chinese teas[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Chinese teas")]

The Chinese plant is a small-leafed bush with multiple stems that reaches a height of some 3 m (9.8 ft). It is native to southeast China. The first tea plant variety to be discovered, recorded, and used to produce tea dates back 3,000 years ago; it yields some of the most popular teas.

_C. s._ var. _waldenae_ was considered a different species, _C. waldenae_ by SY Hu,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-ICS-23) but it was later identified as a variety of _C. sinensis_.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-24) This variety is commonly called Waldenae Camellia. It is seen on [Sunset Peak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunset_Peak_(Hong_Kong) "Sunset Peak (Hong Kong)") and [Tai Mo Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tai_Mo_Shan "Tai Mo Shan") in [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong "Hong Kong"). It is also distributed in the [Guangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guangxi "Guangxi") province.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-ICS-23)

### Indian teas[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Indian teas")]

Three main kinds of tea are produced in India:

- [Assam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assam_tea "Assam tea"), from the var. _assamica_ plant, comes from the near sea-level heavily forested northeastern section of India, the state of [Assam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assam "Assam"). Tea from here is rich and full-bodied. The first tea estates of India was established in Assam in 1837. Teas are manufactured in either the _orthodox_ process or the _CTC_ process.
- [Darjeeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darjeeling_tea "Darjeeling tea"), from the var. _sinensis_ plant, is from the cool and wet [Darjeeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darjeeling "Darjeeling") highland region, tucked in the foothills of the [Himalayas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalayas "Himalayas"). Tea plantations could be at altitudes as high as 2,200 m (7,200 ft). The tea is delicately flavored, and considered to be one of the finest teas in the world. The Darjeeling plantations have three distinct harvests, termed 'flushes', and the tea produced from each flush has a unique flavor. First (spring) flush teas are light and aromatic, while the second (summer) flush produces tea with a bit more bite. The third, or autumn flush gives a tea that is lesser in quality.
- [Nilgiri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilgiri_tea "Nilgiri tea") is from a southern region of India almost as high as Darjeeling. Grown at elevations between 1,000 and 2,500 m (3,300 and 8,200 ft), Nilgiri teas are subtle and rather gentle, and are frequently blended with other, more robust teas.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

## Health effects[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Health effects")]

Main article: [Health effects of tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_effects_of_tea "Health effects of tea")

Although health benefits have been assumed throughout the history of using tea as a common beverage, no high-quality evidence shows that tea confers significant benefits.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-medline-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-nccih-26) In [clinical research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_research "Clinical research") over the early 21st century, tea has been studied extensively for its potential to lower the risk of [human diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_diseases "Human diseases"), but none of this research is conclusive as of 2017.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-medline-25)

## Biosynthesis of caffeine[[edit source](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Camellia_sinensis&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Biosynthesis of caffeine")]

[Caffeine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caffeine "Caffeine"), a molecule produced in _C. sinensis_, functions as a [secondary metabolite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_metabolite "Secondary metabolite") and acts as a natural [pesticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide "Pesticide"): it can paralyze and kill herbivorous insects feeding on the plant.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-insecticide-27) Caffeine is a purine alkaloid and its biosynthesis occurs in young tea leaves and is regulated by several enzymes.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camellia_sinensis#cite_note-29) The biosynthetic pathway in _C. sinensis_ differs from other caffeine-producing plants such as [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee "Coffee") or [guayusa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilex_guayusa "Ilex guayusa"). Analysis of the pathway was carried out by harvesting young leaves and using reverse transcription [PCR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction "Polymerase chain reaction") to analyze the genes encoding the major enzymes involved in synthesizing caffeine. The gene _TCS1_ encodes caffeine synthase. Younger leaves feature high concentrations of TCS1 transcripts, allowing more caffeine to be synthesized during this time. Dephosphorylation of xanthosine-5'-monophosphate into [xanthosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xanthosine "Xanthosine") is the committed step for the xanthosines entering the beginning of the most common pathway. A sequence of reactions turns [xanthosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xanthosine "Xanthosine") (9β-D-ribofuranosylxanthine) into [7-methylxanthosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=7-methylxanthosine&action=edit&redlink=1 "7-methylxanthosine (page does not exist)"), then [7-methylxanthine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-methylxanthine "7-methylxanthine"), then [theobromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theobromine "Theobromine") (3,7-dimethylxanthine), and finally into caffeine (1,3,7-trimethylxanthine).
