## Publications as free as the air we breathe

First, let's start with the free stuff. 

You can find hundreds of free eBooks and thousands of the scientific papers. In fact, there is more quality reading material out there than you can read even if you started today and did nothing else. 

Yes, there are plenty of science books that are north of $50 per copy, but most that is written is free. For books, they are free because the author is long dead and the copyright term has expired, this happens 25 to 100 years depending on the country.

Here is a world map of **copyright term after authors death** by [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries%27_copyright_lengths):

![World Map of Copyright after death](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/World_copyright_terms.svg)

Then, there are plenty of authors who simply want to share their knowledge and make a mark on the culture as a whole. For the aspiring young people it is far more important to **make a name for themselves** than to make a couple cents per copy sold. Of course, this may change in the future when the "LIKES" will be associated with the obligatory $0.02 **micropayment**. I definitely hope this will come soon, as "the talk is cheap, put your money where your mouth is" rule would separate the kernels from the chaff. The reason the idea is not catching is because the "likes" are feeding the databases and the machine learning algorithms of the content provides and the welfare of the authors is secondary, but this is a subject for the future chapter.



### eBooks

My favorite website for free eBooks is [OpenCulture.com](http://www.openculture.com/free_ebooks), advertising "**800 Free eBooks for iPad**". I took me a couple of hours, but I ended up collecting hundreds of classics, some of which I always planned to read. 

I was surprised to find that [Amazon.com](https://www.amazon.com/free-kindle-books/s?k=free+kindle+books) has quite a lot free Kindle books that you can buy with a single click for $0.00.

I already had a few computer eBooks I have bought in bulk from [O'Reilly](https://www.oreilly.com/programming/free/). 

Finally, I have [Kindle Unlimited](https://www.amazon.com/Browse-Kindle-Unlimited-Books/b?node=9069934011) subscription for $9 per month for books I cannot get for free, there are over 70,000 titles is you believe the marketing.



### Scientific Papers

The next thing I am reading in bulk are scientific papers. Well, to say I am "reading" them is a bit of a stretch, most are written to be totally incomprehensible. I guess the authors are cryptic to look smarter, or they add unnecessary bulk and overuse the thesaurus to cover the lack of any real content, or the horrendous writing is caused simply by the inability to formulate thought in a simple to read way. 

I hope the academic advisors and the peer reviewers will start taking the user experience (UX), or rather **reader experience**, into consideration and send the papers back to the authors with note "**Please rewrite to make your message clear and concise**".

My favorite place to look for computer science papers is [PapersWithCode.com](https://www.paperswithcode.com/search?q_meta=&q=machine+learning), but there are many university sites and the magazines such as [Nature](https://www.nature.com/collections/ceabbdbhhe?utm_source=bing&utm_medium=cpc&utm_content=null&utm_campaign=SREP_4_AW02_GL_Top100_2019_Overall&utm_term=scientific%20articles).

In a day you can collect more scientific papers, in PDF format, that you can process in a year worth of reading.



### Web Articles and Blog Posts

Finally, very often, I find a good web article that is too long to read at the moment. Usually, I print it to the PDF and save for later reading.



## Summa Summarum 

In the end, I have ended up with about 1400+ titles, and until recently, I had no real good way to organize them.

I have tried many things, such as printing the PDFs, reading and highlighting, but it is inconvenient and wasteful.

I continue reading and annotating on my laptop with **PDF Reader Pro**, but after the whole day at work, my back is complaining.

I needed a solution that works in the off time.

## Calibre

The first piece of necessary software is Calibre. Calibre holds all my downloaded eBooks and PDFs.  I usually use it to **rename the papers, tag them, and update the authors**. It allows me to convert the book format, and manage the transfer to Kindle reader.



## Kindle Paperwhite

I have gotten (myself) a Kindle eBook reader for father's day. It is a great pocket device to carry everywhere and read the books in total darkness as well as in the bright sunlight when with kids in the the waterpark. The batteries last forever and Kindle is able to play my 300+ audiobooks via a Bluetooth speaker.

Unfortunately, **Kindle Paperwhite does not do science papers**. The PDFs, thanks to the common, idiotic 2-column format, are very difficult to read and the conversion to eBook format ends up being a catchup spaghetti with meatballs.



## iPad Pro 

A few years ago, my wife has purchased the 12-inch screen iPad Pro to draw. Since then, I inherited the iPad as my "cafe" device with ambitions to write on it. Well, I found that iPad Kindle is a perfect eBook and PDF reader thanks to a large and crisp touchscreen display. 

You can email the files to iPad Kindle. To do so, click on "MORE" > Settings > "SEND-TO-KINDLE EMAIL ADRESS" @kindle.com.

The easier way is to connect iPad using USB cable, on MacOS open the Finder, your iPad should be showing as a drive, then go to Files tab > Kindle, and drag all your files to that folder. The books will show up in Kindle app within seconds.



## Summary

I hope you found my observations useful. Happy reading! In the near future I plan to write about how I read the scientific papers.
