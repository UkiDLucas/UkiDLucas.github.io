# Introduction

# Cultures of Okinawa and Japan — Reflections  
沖縄文化と日本文化 • 省察 • 著 宇氣

The three years I spent in Okinawa, Japan, as a young man were so important to me that I call them my formative years. 
During that period, I studied Cultural Anthropology of East Asia, specializing in Japanese Studies. 
My studies, after returning to the United States, never ended, and over the last 30 years, I have come to understand many concepts I did not grasp in Okinawa. I have read countless books and written many blog posts on the subject. I strongly feel the spirit of the place and a deep longing for its people, even if today no one around me understands what I mean.  

I wrote this book for myself to remember, and for my daughters, if in the future they care to understand me.  

Most of all, I write to the place itself. The place is not an abstract idea or just a name. Every coral reef, every mountain, and every waterfall holds a sacred spirit called kami, which I want to respect and celebrate in this book.  

Thank you to the people I knew back then. You were the kindest people I have met in my whole life. To the people I did not know, thank you for putting up with us foreigners, and sorry for any trouble we caused.  

I want to thank my teacher, Dr. Turner-Sacher, who steered me onto the path of anthropology. You changed my life for the better.  

My interest is in native cultural anthropology. I will not write about wars or the terrible Battle of Okinawa, nor about American influence on Japanese culture during the post-war occupation.  

I want to capture the delicate nuance and warmth that I feel when I think of the culture of those islands.

# Shotokan Karate - Early years

I joined a local Shotokan karate (松濤館空手) dōjō (道場) when I was about eleven, although I no longer remember the exact age. Józef Rączka, my sensei (先生), was a friend of my father, who left for the United States around that time. Sensei Rączka’s last name actually means “hand,” or _te_ (手) in Japanese. Back then he held a fourth dan (四段), and today he is a seventh dan master (七段) and one of the most respected European karate judges.

Our sensei (先生) was intense. His kata (形) were beautiful and dynamic, his instruction thoughtful, precise, and ruthless. We repeated each movement until there were puddles of sweat beneath every one of us. He struck our arms, legs, and stomachs to test whether our muscles were tight and our stances firm. We feared him and loved him. Even now, forty years later, I have muscle memory of every stance, punch, and block.

At the same time, I was a competitive sprinter, and my name might still be on the old school gym leaderboard. All that training made Marine Corps boot camp feel easy.

During those years, I learned the etiquette and hundreds of Japanese terms, since all nomenclature in the dōjō (道場) was used strictly in Japanese. I developed a deep appreciation for the culture, and like most boys my age, I loved samurai (侍) films.

# Early Paths That Shaped Me

Before arriving in Okinawa, several experiences prepared me for what I would eventually learn there. Karate was the first. It introduced me to routine, respect, and the quiet discipline of repeating movements until the body moved without hesitation. In the dōjō (道場), I learned early forms of etiquette, bowing, and focus. I also tried za-zen (座禅), even though seated meditation never suited me. Still, it taught me that steadiness of mind was something people trained for, much like stances or breathing.  

My time in the Marine Corps reinforced many of the same ideas. The training was direct and demanding. It taught composure, responsibility, and doing each task with intention. I recognized that these values overlapped with the warrior mindset I had already encountered through budō (武道).  

I was also drawn early on to the lives of Zen monks and the history surrounding them. Their endurance, attention, and sense of purpose stayed with me as I read more about their world. I saw similar threads in the roots of Okinawan karate and its connection to older Chinese and Shaolin practices. None of this made me a scholar, but it gave me a background that helped me pay attention once I reached Japan.  

While living in Okinawa, I sometimes visited a Buddhist temple on quiet Saturday mornings. These visits were simple: incense, morning light, and a brief moment of calm. They left a lasting impression. After I returned to the United States, I slowly found my own balance between what I learned from Zen and what I appreciated in Greek Stoicism. Both offered steady ways to move through life with clarity and restraint. Over time, they naturally shaped my routines, without becoming a formal system.  

These early influences did not define me, but they prepared me to understand Okinawa with a clearer mind and more patience.


# Okinawa, the Land That Formed Me
11/16/25 12:30 PM

For part of my time in Okinawa, I was fortunate to live off-base near Naha. There was a family-owned neighborhood grocery store just a short walk from our apartment. In the mornings I would stop there for the warm steamed rice buns (manju, 饅頭) they kept behind the glass near the counter. Some were filled with sweet red bean (anko, 餡子), others with pork (buta-man, 豚まん).

The mama-san (ママさん) who ran the place greeted every customer with the same gentle irasshaimase (いらっしゃいませ) while bowing slightly, as if welcoming someone important. She wrapped my rice buns in neat paper packages, folding the edges with slow and deliberate movements that felt almost ceremonial. When she handed them to me with both hands, she bowed again.

In neither Poland nor the United States had I ever been treated with such quiet care over something so small. These simple morning interactions became my first real lessons in Okinawan kindness and Japanese culture and became a benchmark for how people should act.


# Okinawa Hiji Falls


I have regularly visited Hiji Falls since 1995, when I lived in Okinawa for three years. It was long before they built wooden walkways and official trails at Hiji Falls. It was just me and a faint footpath weaving through the jungle, slick with mud, cool from the river in a dense, subtropical foliage.  
  
On so many times, I’d wander to the falls barefoot, letting the warm, damp earth of the path cushion each step. I would prefer to go there early in the morning. Centipedes crawled on the ground, and large banana spiders had their 6-foot diameter nets spun across the path. I quickly learned to respect, but not fear, them. I do not remember ever being bitten by mosquitoes. Sometimes, the path was over river stones. The falls were deep in the hills. By the time I reached the waterfall, the outside world no longer mattered. There was just the steady pulse of crashing water, echoing off the rocks and trees. Below the falls, there was a 26-foot deep pool of water.  
  
My favorite ritual was sitting directly beneath the 85-foot waterfall, letting the water pound across my shoulders, almost like a half-wild meditation. It reminded me of those Buddhist monks taking in the flow of nature’s power and beauty. In that moment, time blurred the present and the past. I could stay there for minutes or hours, carried by the waterfall’s steady drumbeat and the hush of the jungle around me.  
  
I am writing this after I left Okinawa years ago, but whenever I think back on Hiji Falls, I can feel longing for the beauty and memories I have left there. It’s as if that quiet magic, hidden under the canopy, became a part of me—and though the trail may be more civilized now, I’ll always treasure the raw, unfiltered way I first experienced it.

# Okinawa - land of the happy immortals
# Okinawa: land of the happy immortals

I was most fortunate to live in Okinawa, Japan, for three years in my twenties. I was also very happy to study the cultural anthropology of East Asia while living there. It did not help my career as much as an MBA, a computer science degree, or a master’s in electrical engineering would have, but it shaped my life in a way I consider more meaningful.

When I arrived in Japan, all I knew was that karate, which I had already practiced for many years, came from Okinawa. I also already had a strong appreciation for Japanese culture at that time.

In Okinawa, I had the opportunity to witness the art of ikebana (flower arrangement) and the tea ceremony in settings of traditional Japanese wood architecture. I learned a lot about Buddhism at a local temple, fell in love with Zen, made friends with a katana blacksmith (another story), and, of course, experienced Matayoshi-ryu Kobudo.

Lifestyle in Okinawa was magical too. Outside of the southern part of the island where Naha is located, life was rural, with fields and houses in the valleys by the ocean, surrounded by lush tropical mountains. I learned that Okinawans live some of the longest lives in the world. There was no hurry, no “industrial diseases”; people went about their lives with a smile and good company.

Unlike in Poland of my childhood, people were polite and society-oriented. Not communists, just genuinely good neighbors. Adults exercised naturally, walking, cycling, or practicing martial arts—something I never saw growing up. Karate itself developed there, when local families, often of noble lineage, preserved and taught their own fighting styles.

The ocean air was clean, free from industrial pollution, and daily work still relied on hand tools and steady movement. Unlike in the U.S., people didn’t chase “fat-free” diets that do more harm than good. At a small, family-run izakaya (居酒屋), which was more a neighborhood pub than a restaurant, Mama-san would serve a hearty bowl of Okinawa soba (沖縄そば): noodles in pork broth with katsuobushi (鰹節, bonito umami), kelp, and vegetables, and she would offer something more substantial to drink alongside it.

I decided to reconnect with my formative past, so I started training again in Okinawan Shobudo karate, practicing arts, and re-reading _The Okinawa Program_ longevity book.

# 03.e A pottery kiln in the Okinawa jungle

Published: Mon, 24 Nov 2025 05:12:00 +0000

Original: https://ukidlucas.blogspot.com/2025/11/kiln.html

---

One of my most lasting cultural impressions of Japanese aesthetics came not from a museum or a famous site but from an unexpected moment in the Okinawan mountains. We were driving along muddy back roads in my old Toyota 4Runner, wandering without much plan. The hills were covered in thick jungle, and the trails looked as if they had not seen many vehicles. When the trees opened, we found a place that felt as if it had stepped out of another century.

Built directly into the hillside was a large kiln. The structure blended with the earth around it, half-buried and half-exposed. Scattered nearby were pieces of pottery, some leaning against stones, others arranged with no clear pattern. Everything looked simple and honest. Nothing was colorful, polished, or prepared for visitors. Nothing hinted at tourism or display. It was a working site, or it had been one not long before.

Next to the kiln was a wooden Japanese-style workshop. The boards were worn from years of use. Inside, the floor was dark and uneven. In the center of the room was a square hearth in the floor, with an iron hook holding a kettle above it. On the raw-wood shelves were stacks of pottery, all earthy and unglazed. They looked made for daily use, not for decoration. It was impossible to tell if the place was abandoned or simply resting on a quiet weekend. We did not touch anything. We stood there quietly and absorbed it with respect.

That memory stayed with me for the rest of my life. It was my first encounter with the deeper layer of Japanese aesthetics, the quiet side that does not announce itself. The place taught me more about wabi-sabi (侘寂), shibui (渋い), and the natural simplicity of Japanese craft than anything I had read or would later study. It showed me that beauty can be found in things that are used, worn, and shaped by hand. There is a calm dignity in objects made slowly, without the intention of impressing anyone.

Today, places like this still exist. Japan has modern cities, but many old pottery villages have survived with their character unchanged. Some are active, some are semi-abandoned, and some operate quietly on the edge of the tourist map. When I return to Japan, I would like to visit them.

In Okinawa, Yachimun no Sato (やちむんの里) in Yomitan is perhaps the closest to what I saw. It has shops, but behind the storefronts are hillside kilns built the traditional way and workshops that look untouched for decades. In Naha, the old back streets of Tsuboya (壺屋) still hide narrow alleys and kilns from the Ryukyu era.

On the mainland, places like Shigaraki (信楽) and Bizen (備前) preserve the feeling of old pottery towns where time moves slowly. Kilns built into hills, wood-fired ovens climbing a slope, wooden workshops with dirt floors, and shelves lined with simple clay work. These places are not staged to look old. They are old.

Visiting them today as a tourist is different from stumbling across a quiet kiln in the Okinawan jungle, but it is worthwhile. The same quiet, the same tools, the same natural shapes. The same sense of stepping briefly into another time.

I would like to go back and see these places, not for nostalgia but to understand more deeply what I saw when I was young. Back then, I did not know the words for the aesthetics that shaped that moment. Now I do. And the memory has only grown more important over time.

# Aruki kata (歩き方) walking meditation

 **Finding Zen in Motion: The Power of Active Meditation**

---

Despite my former karate training, I’ve never been able to sit still for long in traditional za-zen meditation.  The concept of sitting in one place for extended periods just doesn’t resonate with me. Instead, I’ve found my peace in movement, specifically through long walks in the forest. 

Active meditation isn’t a new concept; it’s been practiced for centuries by various cultures. 

My inspiration came from my studies in Cultural Anthropology, particularly the Monks of Mount Hiei in Japan. 

These Tendai monks, known as the "Marathon Monks," engage in kaihōgyō 回峰行 (circling the mountain), a challenging practice that involves walking great distances over long periods (for example 84km per day for 100 days). Their journey is not just physical but deeply spiritual, emphasizing the connection between movement and mindfulness. It is also related to Fudō Myōō (不動明王) to which the temple I attended in Okinawa was dedicated.

I would love to write a whole article about them, if you are interested, as they are amazing.

Stepping into the forest, the trail ahead of me is not just a path but a gateway to a deeper state of awareness. As I walk, I focus on my breath, the texture and the smell of often wet Pacific Northwest moss and ferns, in fact very similar to that of mountains in Japan. Even the trails I frequent on Tiger Mountain hold a special connection for me. They remind me of the tiger in the Shotokan karate emblem and the evergreens that Master Gichin Funakoshi loved so much.

Each step is a mantra, each breath a moment of reflection. 

As a former Marine, I also find a parallel in the string of knots (550 cord) we used, which is similar to Buddhist beads (type of rosary). Each knot, much like each bead, is the distance traveled, number of paces, serves as a point of focus, a reminder of discipline, and a tool for mindfulness.

Walking, usually, three miles in the forest allows me to disconnect from the chaos of daily life. It’s a time to let my thoughts flow freely, to find solutions to problems. 

I wouldn't be a proper Zen Buddhist, if I did not mention that the favorite trail is named "Poo-poo" trail. Yes, there is time to get lofty, but there are dog shit bags on the side of the trail, too. Both are equally enlightening.

I notice the way the sunlight filters through the leaves, especially after the rain, the songs of birds, and the scent of cypress tree in the air. These sensory experiences ground me, helping me to stay anchored in the present.
Sometimes I sit on the trunk of the fallen tree, where the sun comes over thru the canopy. These are my happy moments. Moments of reflection.

So, the next time you feel overwhelmed or in need of a mental reset, consider taking a walk. Embrace the journey, not the destination, find your rhythm, and let the world around you guide you to a place of peace and tranquility. Remember, meditation is not about how you sit; it’s about how you find your center, and for some of us, that center is found in motion.

# Aruki-kata (歩き方), The Quiet Art of Walking

Created on: 2025-12-02 at 09:20

When I visited my parents recently, I noticed how much time they spend sitting. They live in a clean, walkable neighbourhood with fresh air and nature around them, yet most of the day is spent in easy chairs passively watching TV. It is stillness, but not the kind that strengthens the body or exercises the mind.   
  
In Japan, the idea of movement is part of the system and habits. People walk to the train station, to the store, to school, and to visit friends. Even when the distance is not short, walking is the default. This habit begins early and continues into old age. There is no special meaning to it. It is just daily life. The Japanese call this natural way of walking aruki kata (歩き方), the manner of walking. The idea is simple: keep the body in motion whenever possible.  
  
When I lived in Japan, I learned that the art of movement is different from that in the West, where we sit or move as little as possible, then go to the gym and stress the body. In Okinawa, movement was part of everything. People walked to the fields or along the coast. Elderly men and women walked slowly in the mornings, greeting neighbors. They did not schedule fitness. They simply lived in a way that kept them strong. Even today, when I read about Okinawan longevity, I am reminded that their long lives were shaped not only by intense training but also by everyday movement, sunlight, simple food, and community.  
  
Walking has a cultural weight in Japan. The train system supports this lifestyle. You walk to the station, travel as far as you need, then walk the rest of the way. This creates a rhythm in which the body never falls into deep inactivity. The steps are small, but they accumulate. It is one of the reasons older Japanese adults maintain balance, coordination, and mental clarity far longer than people who rely on cars and long hours of sitting.  
  

When I look at my parents, I can see how the lack of movement affects their posture, breathing, and overall cardiovascular strength. Passive sitting offers temporary comfort but slowly erodes the body. Without intentional motion, the body forgets what it is capable of. Even gentle daily walks would help. A ten-minute walk after breakfast, a quiet loop around the neighborhood at sunset. The body does not need intensity. It needs consistency.  
  
In my own life, walking has become a form of active meditation. I wrote earlier about the monks of Mount Hiei, but the principle does not belong only to them. Steady steps, calm breathing, and a quiet mind can be practiced anywhere. The forest trails in the Pacific Northwest give me the same feeling I once had in Okinawa. Moss, rain, silence. A place where walking becomes time to think and rejuvenate.  
  
If there is a lesson here, it is that longevity is less about supplements or perfect diets than about daily rhythm. Standing up. Moving. Using the legs, balancing. Breathing fresh air, watching sunrises and sunsets.  
  
I hope my parents will rediscover this and form new habits. A short walk each day is not a significant change, but it can shift the entire atmosphere of a life. Strength returns slowly. Mood improves. The world feels larger than the space between a chair and a television. It is a gentle way to return to being part of the world instead of merely observing it.  
  
Walking is the simplest practice I know. It requires no equipment, preparation, or instruction. Just a path and a few clear minutes. It is a good way to begin again.


# Nankuru Naisa (なんくるないさ)

Nankuru Naisa (なんくるないさ) is an Okinawan phrase roughly meaning “It will be all right” or “Everything will work out somehow.”

  

It comes from the longer, older expression “Makuto sōke nankuru nai sa” — meaning “If you do the right thing, things will turn out fine.” Over time, people began using just nankuru naisa as a comforting phrase of optimism or faith in life’s natural flow.

  

In spirit, it’s close to que será, será or shikata ga nai (仕方がない) in mainland Japanese, but it carries a warmer, more easygoing island feeling — the Okinawan belief that patience, kindness, and good heart will guide things to harmony.


# Mushin (無心)
# Mushin (無心): The Art of Effortless Flow

#byUkiDLucas  #BeeHiiv #Japan #Zen #Buddhism #public  #Okinawa #Japanese 
2025-03-19

By the time I leave work, my mind feels like a mental battlefield, overloaded and exhausted.

There is such a thing as decision-making overload, and it doesn't matter how big or small those decisions are; you hit your limits.

To recover, I usually listen to something uplifting on the drive home. Sometimes, I manually wash dishes while listening to an audio book or go for a long walk. It works.

I remembered a concept I first encountered in Japan...a term for "emptying your mind" is mushin (無心).

I have lived in Japan and studied the cultural anthropology of East Asia there. Still, over the many years since, I slowly began to forget some of the valuable lessons I learned that shaped who I am today.

I've decided to revisit these lessons because, although Greco-Roman Stoicism has been a central theme of my recent writing, Japanese culture, and Zen Buddhism were foundational influences in shaping who I became as a young person.

When I was young, I thought mushin (無心) literally meant "empty mind," which, of course, I could never achieve. Za-zen meditation felt like torture. I previously wrote about "walking meditation" and the marathon monks of Mount Hiei, monks known for practicing extreme endurance and spiritual discipline through active meditation.

Instead, I've learned that mushin isn't just an empty mind but a **state of effortless flow**, where thoughts arise and dissolve naturally. 

Entire books explore 'flow,' and in Western culture, it’s often associated with 'deep work, but here I literally consider it as effortless thinking, or detachment from the self-conscious effort.

You can experience mushin through active meditation, such as forest walks, where your mind remains a stream of thought but one that shifts its current from work-related concerns to free-flowing ideation.

Think of it like switching train tracks: it’s not about suppressing thought but about allowing it to flow unburdened.

Sometimes, simply having the name (the terminology) can become the key to finding the solution.

Rather than seeking a void through meditation, or worse, trying to relax by watching a silly movie, you can actively practice the art of mushin (無心). Make it part of your daily activities.

I cannot tell you what activity will put you in the state of "flow." It could be playing a musical instrument, programming, bathing, or "just" breathing.  

Perhaps you already practice mushin (無心) without knowing it. The key is not to chase it, but to recognize when it finds you.

Walking has a cultural weight in Japan. The train system supports this lifestyle. You walk to the station, travel as far as you need, then walk the rest of the way. It is one of the reasons older Japanese adults maintain balance, coordination, and mental clarity far longer than people who rely on cars and long hours of sitting.

A ten-minute walk after breakfast, a quiet loop around the neighborhood at sunset. The body does not need intensity. It needs consistency.

In my own life, walking has become a form of active meditation. I wrote earlier about the monks of Mount Hiei, but the principle does not belong only to them. A place where walking becomes time to think and rejuvenate. 

If there is a lesson here, it is that longevity is less about supplements or perfect diets than about daily rhythm. Standing up. Moving. Using the legs, balancing. Breathing fresh air, watching sunrises and sunsets.

I hope my parents will rediscover this and form new habits. A short walk each day is not a significant change, but it can shift the entire atmosphere of a life. Strength returns slowly. Mood improves. The world feels larger than the space between a chair and a television. It is a gentle way to return to being part of the world instead of merely observing it. 

Walking is the simplest practice I know. It requires no equipment, preparation, or instruction. Just a path and a few clear minutes. It is a good way to begin again.

# Wabi-sabi (侘寂)

Wabi-sabi (侘寂) is often described as the beauty of imperfection, age, and quiet simplicity. It is the kind of beauty you do not see when you rush. You notice it only when you slow down enough that small things begin to matter. It is not dramatic or designed to impress. It grows on you with time and attention.

One of the first times I understood wabi-sabi was while spending time in a traditional Japanese house. The tatami room opened toward a small moss garden, separated only by shoji (障子) screens. The light filtered through the paper and softened the entire scene. Outside, a plain wooden platform framed the garden. The wood was gray and oxidized from years of rain and sun. It was not special. It was simply a surface for walking, sitting, or pausing before entering the garden.

The longer I looked, the more I appreciated the details. The grain had become visible with age. The edges were worn smooth. Nothing was polished or perfect. The beauty came from time, use, and the traces of life left in the material. That quiet honesty was my first clear sense of wabi-sabi.

In Okinawa, I saw the same feeling many times. A clay bowl with a small imperfection from the kiln. A wooden bench built by a craftsman, simple but made with care. These objects carried their own history. They were not decorative. They held the marks of use, and that made them better. Growing up in the Polish highlands, I remember old log houses that felt the same way. They were not refined, yet they had a presence you felt the moment you stepped inside.

Sitting in that wooden Japanese house, looking at the moss garden through the softened light, I realized that wabi-sabi is not something you study. It is something you recognize when you are finally still enough to see it. It teaches you to appreciate what is imperfect, aged, and unforced. Life does not need to be arranged to have meaning. Time, patience, and use shape things in ways that no design can.

Shibui (渋い) is related but different: more about quiet refinement and restraint than about age or imperfection. I will return to that idea later. For this moment, the memory of that simple wooden platform and the quiet garden behind the shoji was enough. It showed me how much depth can live in things that ask for nothing.

# Shinrin-yoku (森林浴)
Shinrin-yoku (森林浴) means “forest bathing,” literally “bathing in the forest atmosphere.” It is a simple practice: you walk slowly among trees, breathe naturally, and let the senses settle.

The core idea is not exercise. It is presence.

You let your attention shift from obligations to the feeling of the ground under your feet, the sound of leaves, the moisture in the air, and the quiet rhythm of breathing. Japanese studies since the 1980s show that slow walks in forests reduce stress hormones, lower blood pressure, improve sleep, and restore attention. They also help mood and immune function by exposing the body to natural plant compounds released by trees.

This fits well with your preference for active meditation. Shinrin-yoku is walking without a goal, except to reconnect with natural surroundings. It matches what you describe on Tiger Mountain or in the Pacific Northwest, very similar to forest regions in Japan.


# Shun-kyu (旬久)
In Japanese food culture:

**shun (旬)**  
means the exact moment when an ingredient is at its **deepest flavor, highest nutrition, and natural peak**.  
Fish, vegetables, fruits, even mushrooms each have a short window when they taste the way nature intended.

A few examples:

- **Saba** is best in late autumn when the fat is highest.
    
- **Sanma** is a classic autumn fish with rich, clean flavor.
    
- **Takenoko** tastes best only in early spring, almost days after sprouting.
    
- **Strawberries** and **mikan** have precise seasonal peaks.
    

The idea behind shun is simple:  
eat things **when they are meant to be eaten**, not when forced by greenhouses or long-distance logistics.

This concept shaped Okinawan and Japanese longevity diets. When food is eaten in shun, nutrients are naturally higher and preparation can stay simple. The body recognizes seasonal patterns, and you avoid over-refined or off-season foods that require artificial growth.


# Eating meals with full attention
Eating with full attention is one of the quiet habits that shaped me in Okinawa. Meals were never rushed. Even simple dishes like Okinawa soba or a bowl of rice were handled with care. People sat down, put the phone away, and gave the meal their full attention. It was not a rule, only a way of living. The Japanese phrase itadakimasu (いただきます) captures this. It is not a prayer. It is a small moment of respect for the food, for the people who prepared it, and for the work that went into it. When eaten this way, even an ordinary meal becomes grounding.

When I eat with attention, the mind slows. The body relaxes. The taste becomes clearer. It is the same pattern as ichigyō zammai (一行三昧): do one thing completely. It is also a form of kansha (感謝), quiet gratitude. Respecting food is respecting life, and that habit builds steadiness far beyond the table.

# Fermented Foods
When I think about food and long life, I return to the same simple observation. In Okinawa, people did not talk about nutrition, yet they lived in a way that supported health for decades. Meals were modest, mostly vegetables, beans, seaweed, and fish, with small portions of fermented foods folded into daily life. It was not a trend. It was just the way things were.

I grew up with fermentation in Poland as well. Sauerkraut, pickled cucumbers, kefir. These foods were ordinary, not “functional” or scientific. They support microbial diversity, lower inflammation, and help the body maintain balance.

Large population studies also tell a consistent story. Regions of Japan with steady consumption of fermented soy foods, especially natto, show increased longevity and lower cardiovascular risk. The reasons are not mysterious. These foods are rich in beneficial bacteria, vitamin K2, natural enzymes, and plant-based nutrients that work slowly over time. They fit into a diet built on moderation and steady rhythm. Okinawa was not a natto region, but the principle is the same. Fermented foods support a long life because they work with the body rather than against it.

The government eventually created a category called “functional foods.” Many fermented items were among the first to qualify. It was not a marketing idea but a recognition that traditional diets already contained foods with measurable biological effects. Science did not invent these habits. It confirmed them.

Today, I keep these foods in my own routine. Miso soup, kimchi, pickled vegetables, simple yogurt, and fermented beans appear in small amounts throughout the week. They are easy to prepare, inexpensive, and compatible with modern life. They support digestion and help the body recover from stress. More importantly, they fit the larger pattern I learned in Okinawa: eat modestly, stay active, maintain community, and live with steady habits.

Fermented foods are not a secret. They are evidence of something older and simpler. Cultures that live close to the land discover what helps the body feel grounded. Japan studied these foods in detail, but the knowledge was already present in everyday meals. For me, fermentation is part of that quiet logic. A small, steady support for health, carried from one generation to the next.


# Oyatsu (おやつ)

**Oyatsu, the small pause in the afternoon, reflects more than a snack. It shows how Japanese daily life balances work, energy, and simple pleasure.**

Oyatsu (おやつ) literally began as **oyatsu-doki**, the “eighth hour” of the old Edo clock system. In that system, the day was divided into unequal temporal hours, and the eighth hour aligned with mid-afternoon. People working in fields, shops, or along travel roads would stop briefly for something small, usually sweet or easy to carry. The custom stayed even after the time system disappeared.

In modern Japan, oyatsu at roughly three in the afternoon is a modest pause. It is not a meal. It is a reset. The food itself can be anything from fruit to senbei, a manju filled with anko, a piece of sweet potato, or something baked. For children, it often becomes a small ritual after school. For adults, it remains a quiet break in the middle of long hours, especially in office culture where continuous work is common.

From an anthropological lens, oyatsu reflects the Japanese approach to **rhythm** rather than indulgence. Many cultural patterns favor small, predictable breaks that stabilize the day. It also fits within the wider idea of **moderation** that shaped Okinawan and mainland habits alike, where health is supported by pacing food intake rather than maximizing portions. Literature also preserves the feeling of oyatsu. The mood is never extravagant. It is always gentle and grounded, something that fits naturally inside the day.

What stands out is how oyatsu carries **emotional neutrality**. It is not a reward, not an escape, not a celebration. Just a small act that reconnects a person with their own pace. Oyatsu is not about the food, but about the small window of rest. A cultural gesture, almost invisible, yet deeply tied to how people sustain themselves through the day.

# Awari wa hajimari (終わりは始まり)

The phrase awari wa hajimari (終わりは始まり), “the end is the beginning,” has become something of a quiet guide in my life. I did not adopt it through study or philosophy but through the rhythm of daily living. Over time, I began to sense that what I thought of as chores or routines were actually small cycles of completion and renewal. Making my bed each morning, for instance, is not merely the end of sleep but the first calm gesture of the new day. Cooking and cleaning have become one continuous act — creating, then restoring harmony. Even the warm water and slow rhythm of washing dishes after dinner bring a kind of stillness I never find in a dishwasher’s hum. In these simple motions, I began to feel what the Japanese call mujo (無常), the impermanence that gives life its quiet texture.

  

When I cook, I open jars of spices one by one, inhaling each aroma before deciding what belongs in the pot. I never buy pre-mixed blends, because each spice has its own character and spirit. This small ritual is a moment of ichigo ichie (一期一会) — “one time, one meeting.” The scent of cumin, the sharpness of ginger, the warmth of cinnamon, each combination will never appear again exactly the same way. It is a conversation between nature and my senses, a way of meeting the present moment through something as ordinary as soup. I have come to understand that mindfulness does not require meditation cushions or temples; it happens in the kitchen, in the steam rising from a pot.

  

In Okinawa, where I once lived, this way of being seemed to exist naturally. The people there often said nankuru naisa (なんくるないさ) — “things will work out if you live sincerely.” It was not laziness or avoidance, but trust in the natural order of life. They treated their surroundings with quiet respect, believing that kami (神), the spirits of Shinto, reside in all things — the house, the tools, the garden, the wind. I feel this too. When I handle my kitchen knife or clean the counter, I sense I am tending to more than objects; I am honoring the spirit of the place. The home becomes a living jinja (神社), a personal shrine of gratitude.

  

I have also found this awareness in my work with tools, both physical and digital. I love Japanese joinery, not only for its precision but for the respect it shows toward wood and craftsmanship. The carpenter’s philosophy, shokunin kishitsu (職人気質), resonates with me as deeply as any teaching of Zen. To sharpen a chisel before beginning work is not preparation; it is part of the work itself. In the same way, I see software architecture — the structure before the code — as the sharpening of my craft. A well-tuned tool or a well-structured program allows the spirit of the task to flow without resistance. In both, mastery is less about performance and more about readiness.

  

These ideas have merged for me into a kind of personal dō (道) — a way of life. Whether I am practicing karate, writing code, or tending to a small bonsai, I feel the same rhythm. Each motion is shugyō (修行), daily discipline that refines not only skill but character. Each action ends and begins at once. The repetition is not dull; it is liberating. There is no “done,” only continuous practice.

  

Over time, awari wa hajimari has changed how I see everything. The end of the day, the last spoon washed, the final line of code written — none of these are true endings. They are bows, gestures of gratitude before the next beginning. This awareness brings a calm satisfaction, a kind of chōwa (調和), harmony between what I do and who I am. Life becomes less about achieving and more about participating in its cycles — the quiet flow of beginnings, endings, and the spaces between.


# Ichigyō zammai (一行三昧)

Ichigyō zammai, written as 一行三昧, is a pattern visible across many Japanese arts.

The phrase began in Buddhist practice, where it described complete absorption in a single line of scripture. Over time, it became a wider cultural pattern that shaped craft, ceremony, martial arts, and even daily routines. 

  

The idea is simple: it asks for complete attention to one act, and its presence in so many Japanese traditions shows how deeply it combines discipline and beauty.  
  
In woodcraft, the spirit of the concept is evident in how an artisan handles tools and materials. Consider the intricacy of a complex joint with no nails or glue, which is treated as a complete world while it is being done. There is no rush (急がず, isogazu) toward the next step. Each action begins only when the previous one has settled (間, ma). Workshops feel calm because the work is paced by steady attention rather than by urgency. This rhythm gives the finished object its quiet precision.

  
The tea ceremony expresses the same ideal through ritual. Every motion has a beginning and an end, and the host moves through them with a mind anchored in the present. The placement of the tea bowl, the handling of the ladle, and the final act of offering tea to a guest are carried out without division of thought. This unbroken attention makes the room feel still, and it allows everyone involved to enter a shared space of respect.  
  
Ikebana shows how the idea extends into the aesthetics of nature. The arranger handles one stem at a time, sensing how each choice affects balance and movement. The work does not depend on the number of flowers but on the clarity with which each element is placed. The result carries a sense of harmony because it reflects a mind that has not been pulled in multiple directions.  
  
Martial arts carry this principle into motion. Repeating the action precisely leads to "muscle memory" and eventually to "no mind" (無心, mushin) execution.

  
Every day of life reflects the same pattern. Cooking, cleaning, and studying become more grounded when attention is given to what is directly in front of the hands. Simple routines gain clarity. Even conversation becomes more meaningful when listening is not divided. The culture carries a subtle belief that one action, done wholeheartedly, builds a more composed life.  
  
Ichigyō zammai is not a heroic discipline. It does not require special training or dramatic commitment. It appears in ordinary acts across many domains, shaping them into something attentive and balanced. Through repeated presence in craft, ceremony, martial practice, and work, it becomes a quiet foundation for how Japanese culture approaches effort. The idea encourages a life in which a single moment, lived clearly, is enough to guide the next.

## The origins of self-defense arts

The spiritual origins of this story date back a few thousand years, but for now, I will have to skip the Vedas, India, Buddha's life, and start with Bodhidharma (Daruma, 達摩, だるま), who, among others, in the 6th Century brought Hindu-derived Buddhist culture, meditation, concepts of "chi" energy qigong (氣功), and breathing techniques from India to China. 
## Chinese Shaolin Kung-Fu

Over time, Buddhism has established itself in China alongside Buddhist monasticism, asceticism, and rigorous Zen practice.

## Okinawa’s native fighting style

When I think of Okinawa’s martial identity, I see it not as a mere branch of Chinese Kung Fu but as a native expression of the island’s way of life. While Okinawa received influence from China, its people reshaped those teachings through the realities of their geography and daily existence. Weapons bans, narrow coral paths, and the need for practical self-defense transformed imported movements into something uniquely Okinawan, efficient, fluid, and rooted in natural rhythm.

The first distinguishing trait lies in the _circular block_ age-uke (上受け, あげうけ). In Chinese boxing, many defenses are angular or direct, relying on intercepting the incoming strike. Okinawan practitioners refined these motions into continuous arcs that redirect energy instead of colliding with it. This principle of circular deflection, seen throughout Shuri-te (首里手, しゅりて) and Naha-te (那覇手, なはて), became one of the signatures of Okinawan movement using softness to create strength.

Another expression of this philosophy is _ryūsui no uke_ (流水の受け,  りゅうすいのうけ), the “flowing water block.” It embodies the idea that resistance wastes energy while redirection conserves it. In kata such as Seipai (十八手, せいはい), the practitioner does not simply block but absorbs and turns the attack aside in a single, fluid gesture. The concept mirrors the natural world surrounding Okinawa, where rivers and waves yield yet endure.

The _neko-ashi-dachi_ (猫足立ち, ねこあしだち), or “cat stance,” reveals another island-born adaptation. Okinawan stances are typically higher and more mobile than those in mainland Japanese or Northern Chinese styles. The cat stance allows for immediate movement in any direction, vital for combat in confined spaces or on uneven coral ground. It symbolizes readiness without tension; balanced, watchful, and responsive.

One of the oldest Okinawan hand techniques is _kake-te_ (掛け手,  かけて), the “hooking” or “hanging hand.” In close quarters, it traps or guides an opponent’s arm while setting up a counterstrike. Found in kata like Rōhai (鷺牌, ろうはい), this motion reflects Okinawan combat’s preference for control rather than collision. Because the island’s people were often forbidden to carry weapons, mastery of such trapping techniques became essential for survival.

The _mawashi-uke_ (回し受け, まわしうけ), often translated as “round block,” exemplifies the harmony between offense and defense. Rather than meeting an attack head-on, the hands circle inward to deflect and counter in one motion. This principle, seen prominently in Goju-ryū (剛柔流, ごうじゅうりゅう) kata such as Tensho (転掌, てんしょう), echoes the rhythm of Okinawan breathing: inhale to receive, exhale to release.

Equally distinct is _uchi-komi_ (打ち込み, うちこみ), the idea of “entering” or closing distance with compact strikes. Okinawan fighting evolved for short-range encounters, not stylized duels. Every motion in Sanchin (三戦, さんちん) embodies this philosophy; tight, efficient, and rooted. The elbows, forearms, and knees replace large swings or leaps, turning defense into immediate counterattack.

Finally, the _nukite_ (抜き手, ぬきて), or “spear hand,” stands as one of the purest symbols of Okinawan precision. It targets vital points with the fingertips, requiring discipline, not force. While versions of this technique exist elsewhere in Asia, Okinawan practice emphasized it as part of daily conditioning, reflecting a belief that skill, not strength, determines victory.

In all these forms, Okinawa’s character is visible: resilient, adaptive, and shaped by necessity. Chinese influence was a spark, but the flame took on the island’s color. The people of Okinawa turned martial study into everyday philosophy—one of balance, respect, and survival without aggression. What emerged from this crucible was not imitation but creation: a living art that breathes with the rhythm of the sea surrounding it.
## Okinawan Chinese Hand (tōde, 唐手)

Originally, people in Okinawa used the word tōde (唐手, とうで, “Chinese hand”).
This term 唐手, is pronounced "karate" in Japanese. Later, in 1930s, the term karate (空手, からて, “empty hand”) became standard in Japanese. As we meet the Karate teachers, we will see many of them visited Fujian province in China and learned various Kung-fu styles. 

# Key historical Karate teachers 
  
## Sakugawa Kanga 1733-1815 Shorin-ryū

Sakugawa Kanga (坂川虎山), the son of a humble farmer practicing, Tsurumichi district of Shuri, would become the architect of Shorin-ryū, the system that birthed modern karate.  
  
Sakugawa’s fate turned when he became a retainer to Peichin Takemoto (a peichin was an Okinawan noble, akin to a European baron). Unlike the samurai of Japan, Takemoto rejected rigid hierarchy: "A man’s worth is measured by his character, not his birth."  
  
Under Takemoto, Sakugawa trained in Okinawan ti (手), using tools like the sai, a three-pronged weapon designed to disable an opponent’s sword. Yet something was missing.  
  
In 1760, Sakugawa embarked on a voyage to Fujian, China, where he sought enlightenment at the Southern Shaolin Temple.   
  
At Shaolin, he found what he sought under Kangryū (康琉), a warrior-monk who taught the fluidity of White Crane Fist (白鶴拳) and the explosive power of the Five Ancestors Style (五祖拳).  
  
Returning to Okinawa, Sakugawa synthesized these arts into a new system.  
His kata Niseishi (二十一手) became the first to blend Chinese techniques with Okinawan circular blocks.  
  
He taught Matsumura Sōkon, who would later codify Shorin-ryū.   
  
Sakugawa died in 1815, his tombstone bearing no grand epitaph, just "The Way of the Karate Man."  
His legacy lives on in the Niseishi kata, still practiced today.

## Matsumura Sōkon 1809–1896 Shorin-ryū

Matsumura Sōkon (松村宗棍) is the founder of Seito (正統, "the orthodox tradition") and a cornerstone of Okinawan karate. As a shizoku (士族, a warrior class akin to samurai), he served three Ryukyu kings: Shō Kōku (尚灝), Shō Iku (尚育), and Shō Tai (尚泰).  
  
His martial arts pedigree is legendary. After training under Wang Ji (王志, Ōji) at the Southern Shaolin Temple in Fujian, China, he studied under Sakugawa Kanga (坂川虎山), the originator of Shorin-ryū. Matsumura synthesized these influences into Shuri-te (首里手), a distinctive blend of Shaolin’s linear strikes and Okinawan circular flows.  
  
His legacy extends through his students, notably Ankō Asato (糸洲安恒), after whom the Ankō-ryū style is named. Matsumura also created foundational kata like Seisan (十三手, "Thirteen Hands"), which emphasized practical combat techniques. Other kata such as Hakkaku (八角, "Eight Corners") and Chintō (鎮東, "Pacify the East") became pillars of modern karate.  
  
Matsumura’s disciplined approach earned him the moniker "Bushi Matsumura"—a title reflecting his samurai-like rigor, uncommon among Okinawan warriors of his time.  
  
## Ankō Itosu 1831–1915 Shuri-te

The year was 1879 when Ankō Itosu (糸洲安恒, 1831–1915) stood before the young king of Ryukyu, Shō Tai (尚泰), and made an unthinkable request: to teach karate in Okinawa’s public schools. At the time, martial arts were forbidden—considered too dangerous for commoners. Yet Itosu argued that karate was more than combat; it was a discipline of the mind and body.

Itosu’s persistence paid off. By 1897, karate was introduced into Okinawa’s education system, marking the first time a martial art had been formalized for mass instruction. His methods were revolutionary: he simplified kata (型) into structured, progressive forms that could be taught to children.

Before Itosu, karate was a closely guarded secret, passed only among nobles and warriors. He changed that by creating the **Pinan (平安)** series—five kata (Pinan Shodan, Nidan, etc.) designed to teach fundamentals like stances, blocks, and strikes in a logical sequence. His philosophy was clear: "Karate is for life, not just fighting."

Itosu also emphasized **kime (決め)**, the concept of "deciding" in combat—focused commitment to every movement. His students, including Chōshin Chibana (the founder of Shorin-ryū Kobudō) and Gichin Funakoshi (who later founded Shotokan), carried his teachings to the world.

Yet Itosu’s greatest legacy was his belief in **karate-dō (空手道)**—the idea that karate is a path of self-cultivation. He once wrote:

_"Karate is like boiling water—if you don’t keep the heat on, it will cool. Practice with sincerity."_

Itosu passed away in 1915, but his influence endures. Today, the Pinan kata remain staples in nearly every karate style, a testament to his vision of martial arts as education.


## Ankō Asato 1827-1906 Ankō-ryū

The year was 1850 when a young Okinawan merchant named Ankō Asato (糸洲安恒) knelt before Matsumura Sōkon in the dim light of a Shuri dojo. Unlike other students, Asato wasn’t born to Okinawan nobility or the warrior class. His family traded silk and herbs between Okinawa and Fujian, China; a life of privilege that left him restless.  
  
Matsumura saw something in the merchant’s son. Asato trained relentlessly, but his approach was different: "Fighting is not only for samurai," he often said. His kata Seipai (十八手) became legendary for its close-range strikes, designed to neutralize an opponent quickly; useful in the crowded streets of Naha.  
  
Asato’s innovations didn’t end there. He developed breathing techniques that harmonized Shaolin’s qi (氣) with Okinawan circular motions. His students called this "the way of breathing"—a precursor to modern ki (気) training. One such student was Chōjun Miyagi, who later founded Goju-ryū (剛柔流). Under Asato’s guidance, Miyagi mastered the Sanchin (三戦) kata, which became Goju-ryū’s cornerstone.  
  
Asato’s legacy extended beyond kata. He taught that karate should be "a way of life," not just a combat technique. His philosophy resonated with Okinawan commoners, who saw martial arts as a means of self-betterment rather than as a means of battlefield dominance. Even today, his Ankō-ryū style lives on at the Shōrinji Temple in Naha, where his disciples have preserved his teachings.  
  
When Asato died in 1906, his grave bore a simple inscription: "The way of karate is endless."  



## Gichin Funakoshi 1868–1957 (Shotokan)

The year was 1922 when a frail, soft-spoken Okinawan schoolteacher named Gichin Funakoshi (船越義珍, 1868–1957) stood before an audience in Tokyo. He was invited to demonstrate karate at a martial arts expo, but few expected much from this elderly man. What happened next changed history.

Funakoshi performed **Heian Shodan (平安初段)**, one of the kata he had helped refine under Ankō Itosu. His movements were precise, yet fluid—unlike the rigid kata of Japanese jujutsu or kendo. The audience was mesmerized.

This moment marked the birth of karate as we know it today. Funakoshi didn’t just introduce a martial art; he transformed it into **karate-dō (空手道)**, emphasizing its philosophical depth. He believed that karate should be a way of life, not just a fighting system.

Funakoshi’s greatest challenge was adapting karate for mainland Japan. He altered kata names to avoid associations with China (e.g., "Tōde" became "Karate") and developed the **dojo kun (道場訓)**, a moral code for students. His writings, such as _Karate-Dō Kyōhan_, became foundational texts.

Yet Funakoshi’s legacy is bittersweet. While he globalized karate, some Okinawans accused him of diluting its essence by catering to Japan’s samurai-influenced martial arts culture. His response was simple:

_"Karate is like hot water—if you don’t keep the heat on, it cools."_

Funakoshi passed away in 1957, but his vision lived on. Today, Shotokan is the world’s most widely practiced karate style—a testament to one man’s belief that martial arts could transcend borders.



## Morihei Ueshiba  1883 – 1969 Aikido

The Hidden Connection – Okinawan Karate, Aikidō, and the Art of Redirection

At first glance, Okinawan karate (空手 / からて) and Aikidō (合気道 / あいきどう) appear to inhabit different worlds. Karate teaches decisive strikes, while Aikidō moves through throws and locks. Yet beneath their surface lies the same current: the principle of circular redirection, of meeting strength not with resistance but with adaptation. Both arts grew from a single truth visible throughout East Asian thought—that harmony triumphs where confrontation fails.

Morihei Ueshiba (植芝盛平 / うえしばもりへい) was born in Wakayama Prefecture in 1883, far from Okinawa, yet his life intersected with the same cultural forces that shaped Okinawan martial traditions. He studied the severe Daitō-ryū Aiki-jūjutsu (大東流合気柔術 / だいとうりゅうあいきじゅうじゅつ) under Takeda Sōkaku, learning subtle joint locks, control of the center, and the timing of connection known as aiki (合気 / あいき). But Ueshiba’s genius was not imitation; it was transformation. He synthesized technique with spiritual philosophy drawn from Shintō (神道 / しんとう) and the Ōmoto-kyō movement, arriving at an art devoted not to victory but to reconciliation.

Although Ueshiba did not study directly in Okinawa, the influence of Okinawan karate reached him through Japan’s martial revival. By the early twentieth century, teachers such as Gichin Funakoshi (船越義珍 / ふなこしぎちん) had brought Okinawan methods to the mainland. Ueshiba observed their emphasis on tai-sabaki (体捌き / たいさばき), the body-shifting movement used to evade rather than block. This principle mirrored his own exploration of tenkan (転換 / てんかん), the pivot step of Aikidō, which receives and redirects an opponent’s momentum in an arc. Where Okinawan masters used circular blocks like mawashi-uke (回し受け / まわしうけ) to turn aside a strike, Ueshiba extended the circle outward into a throw.

The kinship runs deeper than movement. Okinawan karate and Aikidō share an ethical center: self-control as the measure of mastery. In karate, one tempers physical power through rei (礼 / れい), the etiquette of respect. In Aikidō, one seeks musubi (結び / むすび), the spiritual connection that allows conflict to dissolve before it begins. Both descend from the same cultural soil that valued discipline over domination. Even their training halls echo each other—the dōjō (道場 / どうじょう) as a space of purification, where the body learns harmony through repetition.

Ueshiba’s mature art transformed combat into a living meditation. Observers often noted how he moved like water, entering an attack and spiraling it away as though drawn by gravity. This motion resembled the Okinawan ryūsui no uke (流水の受け / りゅうすいのうけ), the “flowing water block,” in which energy never halts but simply changes course. The connection is not of lineage but of principle: both arts discovered that circular motion neutralizes aggression without destruction.

In the postwar years, Aikidō spread beyond Japan, just as karate had decades earlier. The two arts often met on the same tatami (畳 / たたみ) floors, practiced by students who sensed their philosophical kinship. Where karate cultivated decisive finishing technique, Aikidō refined continuous movement. Together they describe the complete rhythm of life—force and yielding, strike and return, beginning and completion.

When Ueshiba died in 1969, his students called him Ō-sensei (大先生 / おおせんせい), the Great Teacher. His final message echoed the ancient Okinawan spirit of harmony: “True budō (武道 / ぶどう) is the loving protection of all things.” The same sentiment lives in every bow at the start of kata and in every breath of Sanchin (三戦 / さんちん). The art of redirection, whether called Aikidō or Okinawan karate, is ultimately the art of returning to balance. Both paths remind us that mastery lies not in overpowering another, but in guiding conflict toward peace.


## Chōjun Miyagi 1888–1953 Goju-ryū

  
The year was 1895 when a young Chōjun Miyagi first entered Ankō Asato’s dojo in Naha. He wasn’t like the other students—no warrior blood ran through his veins, just the quiet determination of a merchant’s son. Asato saw something in Miyagi and took him under his wing.  
  
Under Asato, Miyagi mastered Sanchin, not as a physical exercise but as a meditation. The kata’s slow, deliberate motions taught him that karate was a way of life, not just combat. When Asato died in 1906, Miyagi felt a hole open inside him—but he knew his path wasn’t finished.  
  
At 20, he sailed for Fujian, China, where the monks of the White Crane Fist school recognized his potential. "Your fists carry Okinawa’s spirit," they told him, "but your heart is Chinese." The monks taught him to harmonize harsh strikes with fluid circular motions—a balance he’d later call Goju (剛柔, "hard-soft").  
  
Returning to Okinawa, Miyagi synthesized his knowledge into Goju-ryū. His system wasn’t just about fighting; it was about breathing (ibuki), balance, and the flow of life energy. He taught that karate should be a philosophy, not just a sport.  
  
In 1928, Miyagi made history by demonstrating karate in Tokyo—a move that brought Okinawan martial arts to the Japanese mainland. Yet fame never interested him.  
  
Miyagi’s final years were spent refining his teachings. He emphasized the dantō (段当, "right path")—a way of life rooted in discipline and harmony. When he passed away in 1953, his students carried on his vision.  
  
Today, Goju-ryū stands as a testament to Miyagi’s belief: "Karate is not just a martial art. It is the way of life."

## Chōshin Chibana 1885–1969 Shorin-ryū

The year was 1897 when a young Chōshin Chibana knelt before his master, Ankō Itosu, in the flickering light of a Shuri dojo. The air smelled of crushed coral and shochu, the scent of Okinawa itself. Chibana wasn’t just learning techniques; he was absorbing a way of life.

Itosu had already reshaped karate by creating the Pinan (平安) kata, forms designed to teach discipline, not just combat. But Chibana saw something deeper in Itosu’s teachings: "Karate is life," his sensei would say. When Itosu passed in 1915, Chibana felt the weight of legacy.

In 1933, he opened the Kōbōkan (皇武館) dojo. Unlike Funakoshi, who adapted karate for mainland Japan, Chibana preserved Shuri-te (首里手) in its raw form. His students called it "the old way"—a fusion of Shaolin precision and Okinawan circular flows.

Yet Chibana wasn’t bound by tradition. He insisted that kata were "alive," meant to be adapted. His bunkai (分解) training taught students to apply techniques in real-world situations—a philosophy that would later influence modern Shudōkan.

In 1948, Chibana met Gichin Funakoshi, founder of Shotokan. The two men shared a quiet respect. While Funakoshi brought karate to Japan’s elite, Chibana kept its Okinawan soul intact. Their disagreement wasn’t about style but purpose—Funakoshi saw karate as a global sport; Chibana believed it was a way of life.

When Chibana died in 1969, his students carried on his vision. Today, the Shōrinji Temple in Naha still teaches Kōbōkan style—proof that Shuri-te endures.

## Kanken Toyama  1888–1966 Shudōkan

The Teacher Who United East and West of the Karate World

I often think of Kanken Toyama (富山寛賢 / とやまかんけん) as the bridge between eras—the man who carried the spirit of old Okinawan ti (手 / て) into the modern budō (武道 / ぶどう) world. He was born in 1888 in Shuri, Okinawa, the ancient seat of the Ryūkyū kings, at a time when karate was still a secret discipline practiced behind closed gates. As a youth, Toyama studied under the great masters of the Shuri-te (首里手 / しゅりて) tradition, including Ankō Itosu (糸洲安恒 / いとすあんこう) and Yabu Kentsū (屋部憲通 / やぶけんつう). From them he absorbed the precision of kata and the idea that martial discipline must shape character before it shapes the body.

After graduating from the Okinawa Prefectural Teachers College, Toyama moved to Taiwan in 1924. There, his life took a profound turn. Taiwan was then under Japanese rule but culturally closer to China, and it was in that environment that Toyama immersed himself in Chinese quanfa (拳法 / けんぽう). He trained with masters of Ch’uan Fa and Fujian Crane boxing, studying soft circular deflections, breathing, and the concept of continuous motion. These years in Taiwan transformed his perspective. He began to see that karate and Chinese martial arts were branches of the same ancient tree—the search for harmony between motion, mind, and energy.

When Toyama returned to Japan in 1930, he was already an accomplished martial artist and educator. He opened his dōjō in Tokyo, naming it **Shudōkan (修道館)**—“The Hall for the Study of the Way.” The name expressed his belief that karate was not merely a fighting system but a lifelong path of self-cultivation. He refused to align strictly with any single Okinawan style; instead, he called his art _Dai Nihon Karate-dō_ (大日本空手道 / だいにほんからてどう), “Great Japan Karate Way,” embracing both Okinawan and continental influences.

Unlike many of his contemporaries, Toyama resisted the growing trend of codifying karate into rigid schools. While others declared distinct styles—Shotokan, Goju-ryu, Shito-ryu—Toyama taught that karate should remain a universal practice of principles, not divisions. His curriculum included both traditional kata from Okinawa and new creations inspired by his studies in China and Japan. Among them were the Taikyoku (太極 / たいきょく) series for beginners, the Kyoku (極 / きょく) kata for advanced students, and internal forms such as Penpei (ペンペイ) and Seiru (青流 / せいる) that embodied circular breathing and softness. His synthesis extended to kobudō (古武道 / こぶどう), preserving the use of weapons like bō (棒 / ぼう), sai (鎖 / さい), and tonfa (トンファー).

Toyama’s method emphasized **kaiso no kokoro (開祖の心 / かいそのこころ)**—the founder’s heart—expressed through humility, discipline, and the pursuit of perfection. He believed that “karate begins and ends with courtesy,” a phrase that echoed through the halls of Shudōkan and became a moral compass for his students. His approach to training was rigorous but reflective. He urged practitioners to practice kihon (基本 / きほん, fundamentals) daily, but also to meditate on why a movement exists, not merely how it is performed. To him, karate-dō (空手道 / からてどう) was inseparable from seishin tanren (精神鍛錬 / せいしんたんれん), the forging of the spirit.

In the postwar period, Toyama became one of the senior figures responsible for reestablishing karate in Japan. He founded the **All Japan Karate-dō Federation (全日本空手道連盟 / ぜんにほんからてどうれんめい)** and served as its honorary president. He never promoted the idea of “Shudōkan style” as a brand; instead, he encouraged his students to explore and preserve the full spectrum of karate’s heritage. Many later founded their own lineages—among them Hironori Ōtsuka (Wadō-ryū), Yasuhiro Konishi (Shindō Jinen-ryū), and Masatoshi Nakayama (Shotokan), who drew inspiration from Toyama’s inclusive philosophy.

Toyama passed away in 1966, leaving behind not a rigid system but a living garden of ideas. His students continued to evolve his teaching, spreading Shudōkan karate around the world. Yet his spirit remains best expressed in his motto, still inscribed in many dōjō walls:

**“Shudō wa michi no shūren nari.” (修道は道の修練なり)**  
_“The study of the Way is the training of the Way itself.”_

To study Shudōkan today is to walk that same path—to refine one’s body, temper one’s mind, and harmonize the forces of strength and gentleness that define true karate-dō. Toyama’s legacy endures not as a fixed system, but as an invitation to lifelong polishing, migaki (磨き / みがき), the art of never ceasing to learn.



## Seikichi Toguchi 1917–2003 Shōreikan

The year was 1945, and Okinawa lay in ruins. Among the rubble of Shuri Castle stood Seikichi Toguchi (1917–2003), a man who would become one of karate’s most devoted guardians. At that time, few imagined how deeply he would shape the art of kobudō (古武道).

Toguchi was born in Kunigami Village, a place rich in the traditions of Ryukyu (琉球). His early training began under Chōshin Chibana (知南松釣), a student of Ankō Itosu. But Toguchi’s path diverged when he became one of the last direct pupils of **Chōjun Miyagi (宮城長順)**, founder of Goju-ryū. Under Miyagi, he absorbed the essence of kata like **Sanchin (三戦)** and **Seipai (十八手)**, but he also sought something deeper—the forgotten weapons of Okinawa.

After Miyagi’s death in 1953, Toguchi dedicated himself to preserving Okinawa’s martial heritage. He traveled the island, documenting ancient weapons techniques from masters like **Shōrin Kanryō Higaonna (東恩納寛量)**. His findings became the foundation of **Ryūkyū Kobudō** (琉球古武道), a system often overshadowed by empty-hand karate.

In 1956, Toguchi founded the **Shudokan (修道館)** dojo in Naha. Unlike other schools, Shudokan was a sanctuary for kobudō—teaching bo (棒), sai (鎖), and nunchaku (ヌンチャク) with the same reverence as kata. His philosophy was simple: "Weapons are not tools of violence, but extensions of the body and spirit."

Yet Toguchi’s greatest contribution was his insistence on **bunkai (分解)**, the practical application of kata. He believed that every movement in a form had a combat purpose, even if its origins lay in ancient warfare. His students studied **Kusanku (公相君)** not just as a sequence of motions, but as a living combat system.

Toguchi’s legacy endures today. The Shudokan remains one of the few dojos where kobudō is taught alongside karate. His writings, including his autobiography _Karate-Do: My Way of Life_, emphasize that martial arts are about **personal growth**, not just self-defense.

When Toguchi passed away in 2003, he left behind a system that honored Okinawa’s past while embracing the future. His final words to his students were:

_"Do not seek perfection in technique, but in character."




## Shinpō Matayoshi 1922–1997 Kobudō

The year was 1920, and in the heart of Naha’s cobblestone streets, a young Shinpō Matayoshi (又吉真砲, 1922–1997) began his journey. Unlike most karateka, Matayoshi’s lineage traced directly to Okinawa’s warrior-monks—a connection that would shape his life and legacy.

Matayoshi was born into a family of traditional karate and kobudō practitioners. His father, Shinpō Matayoshi I (又吉真砲), was a student of **Aramburu Kiyohide** and a master of the bo (棒). From him, Shinpō learned the **Matayoshi-ryū Shitō Kensetsu Karate & Kobudō** (又吉流四唐拳剣術), a rare fusion of empty-hand techniques and weaponry.

But Matayoshi’s greatest mentor was **Koshin Ryō (良慶進)**, a man who bridged the gap between Okinawan and Chinese martial arts. Under Ryō, he mastered **Ryūkyū Kobudō**, including the sai (鎖) and tonfa (トンファー). Yet his training didn’t stop there—Ryō sent him to Fujian, China, where he studied **White Crane Fist** under Master Wong Zhi Yang.

Returning to Okinawa, Matayoshi became a guardian of the island’s martial heritage. He founded **Ryūkyū Kobudō Hozon Shinkokai (琉球古武道保存振興会)**, an organization dedicated to preserving ancient weapons techniques. His dojo, **Matayoshi Kōbukai**, taught that kobudō was not just about combat, but about **harmony between mind and tool**.

Matayoshi’s legacy is most evident in his **kata (型)**. Forms like **Rōhai (霊亥)**, originally a Shaolin art, became hallmarks of his style. He also revived lost weapons kata such as **Kusanku Sai (公相君鎖)** and **Chūrin no Kun (中倫の棒)**, ensuring they wouldn’t fade into history.

In his later years, Matayoshi traveled the world, demonstrating kobudō at events like the **All Japan Karate Championships**. Yet his true impact was in Okinawa, where he trained generations of students to see weapons as extensions of themselves.

When Matayoshi passed away in 1997, he left behind a system that honored the past while adapting to the future. His final words were simple:

_"The weapon is not separate from you—it is an expression of your spirit."_




## Masutatsu Ōyama 1923–1994

The year was 1953, and in the streets of Tokyo, a young Okinawan immigrant named Masutatsu Ōyama (大山倍達, 1923–1994) was about to change the world. He had trained under **Gichōfun (義珍)**, a student of Ankō Itosu, and absorbed the **Shōrin-ryū** techniques that defined Okinawan karate. But Ōyama saw something missing—**reality**.

His first test came in 1952 when he challenged boxing champion **Masahiko Kimura (木村政彦)**. The fight ended in a draw, but Ōyama learned a brutal truth: karate needed **full-contact** to prove its worth. Most traditional styles avoided hard sparring, fearing injury or defeat.

Ōyama left Okinawa for Japan and trained relentlessly. He studied **Shōrin-ryū** under **Eiji Yashiro (矢代英治)** and **Gōjū-ryū** under **Sōke Genshin Higaonna (東恩納源信)**. But he sought something more—**a system that worked outside the dojo**.

In 1964, he founded **Kyokushinkai (極真会)**, a style built on tenets: **"Full-contact, no protective gear, and respect through strength."** His students called it **the hard way (kōshū)**. Ōyama believed that true karate wasn’t about winning tournaments, but about **surviving real fights**.

Kyokushinkai’s **kata (型)** remained rooted in Okinawa—Ōyama preserved forms like **Bassai Dai (抜賽大)** and **Sanjin (三戦)**. But his innovation was in **kumite (組手)**, where practitioners fought until one fighter conceded or was knocked out. This approach earned Kyokushinkai both admiration and criticism.

Ōyama’s legacy extends beyond technique. He built **the Ōyama Dojo (大山道場)**, a temple of sweat and pain. His philosophy was simple: **"Karate is not just kicking and punching, but the mastery of oneself."**

When Ōyama passed away in 1994, Kyokushinkai had spread globally. His final words were:

_"Train hard, fight fair, and let your spirit be your guide."_

Today, Kyokushinkai stands as a bridge between Okinawan tradition and modern combat. It proves that karate can adapt while staying true to its roots.


# Complete Shudōkan Kata List

## 1. Taikyoku Shodan (太極初段 / たいきょくしょだん)

**Level:** 10th–9th kyū (white belt)  
**Origin:** Created by Kanken Toyama (inspired by Funakoshi’s Taikyoku series)  
**Stances:** Zenkutsu-dachi (前屈立ち / ぜんくつだち), Kōkutsu-dachi (後屈立ち / こうくつだち)  
**Blocks:** Gedan-barai (下段払い / げだんばらい), Age-uke (上受け / あげうけ)  
**Strikes:** Oi-zuki (追い突き / おいづき), Gyaku-zuki (逆突き / ぎゃくづき)  
**Practical Focus:** Foundation kata teaching linear movement, rhythm, and basic breathing. The first step toward posture and hip connection.

---

## 2. Taikyoku Nidan (太極二段 / たいきょくにだん)

**Level:** 9th–8th kyū (white–yellow belt)  
**Origin:** Toyama line refinement of Shotokan structure  
**Stances:** Zenkutsu-dachi, Kiba-dachi (騎馬立ち / きばだち)  
**Blocks:** Soto-uke (外受け / そとうけ), Uchi-uke (内受け / うちうけ)  
**Strikes:** Oi-zuki, Gyaku-zuki  
**Practical Focus:** Builds confidence in directional turns and rhythm. Reinforces the concept of combining block and counter in a single breath.

---

## 3. Taikyoku Sandan (太極三段 / たいきょくさんだん)

**Level:** 8th–7th kyū (yellow–orange belt)  
**Stances:** Kiba-dachi  
**Blocks:** Shuto-uke (手刀受け / しゅとううけ)  
**Strikes:** Kizami-zuki (刻み突き / きざみづき, jab), Mae-geri (前蹴り / まえげり, front kick)  
**Practical Focus:** Introduces lateral defense and distance control. Emphasizes stability and basic striking flow.

---

## 4. Pinan Shodan (平安初段 / へいあんしょだん)

**Level:** 7th–6th kyū (orange–green belt)  
**Origin:** Itosu lineage (Shōrin-ryū)  
**Stances:** Zenkutsu-dachi, Kōkutsu-dachi  
**Blocks:** Age-uke, Gedan-barai  
**Strikes:** Oi-zuki, Uchi-uke  
**Practical Focus:** Coordination of upper and lower body. Teaches transition between defense and offense through posture and timing.

---

## 5. Pinan Nidan (平安二段 / へいあんにだん)

**Level:** 6th kyū (green belt)  
**Blocks:** Shuto-uke  
**Strikes:** Gyaku-zuki, Yoko-geri (横蹴り / よこげり)  
**Practical Focus:** Strengthens hip rotation and teaches how to generate power from the center. Begins use of side-kick application.

---

## 6. Pinan Sandan (平安三段 / へいあんさんだん)

**Level:** 5th–4th kyū (blue belt)  
**Stances:** Neko-ashi-dachi (猫足立ち / ねこあしだち)  
**Blocks:** Morote-uke (諸手受け / もろてうけ)  
**Strikes:** Tate-zuki (縦突き / たてづき), Uraken-uchi (裏拳打ち / うらけんうち)  
**Practical Focus:** Improves fluid body movement, transition between attack directions, and use of short-range strikes.

---

## 7. Pinan Yondan (平安四段 / へいあんよんだん)

**Level:** 3rd kyū (brown belt)  
**Stances:** Fudō-dachi (不動立ち / ふどうだち)  
**Blocks:** Soto-uke, Nami-gaeshi (波返し / なみがえし)  
**Strikes:** Kizami-zuki, Mawashi-geri (回し蹴り / まわしげり)  
**Practical Focus:** Enhances balance, coordination, and readiness to shift from solid defense to counterstrike in a continuous rhythm.

---

## 8. Pinan Godan (平安五段 / へいあんごだん)

**Level:** 2nd–1st kyū (brown belt)  
**Blocks:** Ude-uke (腕受け / うでうけ), Uchi-uke  
**Strikes:** Sequential combinations with kicks and open-hand techniques  
**Practical Focus:** Integrates all fundamentals. Prepares the body and mind for advanced kata. Teaches awareness of multiple attackers.

---

## 9. Rohai (鷺牌 / ろうはい)

**Level:** Shodan (1st dan)  
**Origin:** Tomari-te lineage, adopted by Toyama  
**Stances:** Neko-ashi-dachi  
**Blocks:** Kake-te (掛け手 / かけて), Shuto-uke  
**Strikes:** Nukite (抜き手 / ぬきて), Empi-uchi (肘打ち / ひじうち)  
**Practical Focus:** Teaches fluid balance, one-legged transitions, and trapping defense. Symbolizes the grace and vigilance of a heron.

---

## 10. Seiru (青流 / せいる, “Blue Flow”)

**Level:** Nidan (2nd dan)  
**Origin:** Kanken Toyama original kata  
**Stances:** Shiko-dachi (四股立ち / しこだち), Sanchin-dachi (三戦立ち / さんちんだち)  
**Blocks:** Mawashi-uke, Tensho (転掌 / てんしょう)  
**Strikes:** Tate-empi (縦肘 / たてえんぴ)  
**Practical Focus:** Develops breath coordination and smooth power transitions. Symbolizes flow and adaptability in combat.

---

## 11. Kyoku Shodan–Rokudan (極初段～六段 / きょくしょだん～ろくだん)

**Level:** Nidan–Yondan (black belt progression)  
**Origin:** Toyama’s structured six-kata series unique to Shudōkan  
**Common Techniques:** Kake-te, Morote-uke, Kizami-zuki, Nage (投げ / なげ, throw), Ushiro-geri (後蹴り / うしろげり, back kick)  
**Practical Focus:** Each Kyoku form increases complexity, combining multiple opponents, height changes, and grappling elements. They form the core of Shudōkan’s advanced technical curriculum.

---

## 12. Empi Iwa (燕飛岩 / えんぴいわ, “Flying Swallow over Rock”)

**Level:** Sandan (3rd dan)  
**Origin:** Toyama adaptation of Empi (燕飛 / えんぴ) from Shotokan  
**Stances:** Zenkutsu-dachi, Kosa-dachi (交差立ち / こうさだち)  
**Blocks:** Uchi-uke, Soto-uke  
**Strikes:** Empi-uchi, Mae-geri  
**Practical Focus:** Sharp vertical movements and explosive direction changes. Builds the feeling of flight and elevation—up, down, and turning mid-motion.

---

## 13. Empi Taki (燕飛滝 / えんぴたき, “Flying Swallow over Waterfall”)

**Level:** Yondan (4th dan)  
**Origin:** Toyama original, derived from Empi Iwa  
**Stances:** Fudō-dachi, Shiko-dachi  
**Blocks:** Circular open-hand deflections  
**Strikes:** Combination elbow, palm-heel, and nukite strikes  
**Practical Focus:** Refines rhythm, breath control, and emotional balance under motion. Symbolizes grace overcoming turbulence.

---

## 14. Penpei (ペンペイ)

**Level:** Yondan (4th dan)  
**Origin:** Fujian White Crane lineage (via Toyama’s studies)  
**Stances:** Sanchin-dachi  
**Blocks:** Mawashi-uke, Tensho  
**Strikes:** Short-range palm and knuckle strikes  
**Practical Focus:** Internal energy development, coordination of breath and motion. Focuses on economy and resilience at close range.

---

## 15. Penpo (ペンポ)

**Level:** Godan (5th dan)  
**Origin:** Continuation of Penpei series  
**Stances:** Higher Sanchin and transitional stances  
**Blocks:** Integrated circular and sweeping motions  
**Strikes:** Combined hand-foot counter sequences  
**Practical Focus:** Symbolic of completeness—defense transforms into continuous flow, emphasizing control and redirection rather than confrontation.

---

## 16. Shimpasho (新破小 / しんぱしょう)

**Level:** Godan–Rokudan (senior black belt)  
**Origin:** Toyama creation, “Small New Breakthrough”  
**Stances:** Compact neko-ashi and shiko transitions  
**Blocks:** Short circular parries  
**Strikes:** Rapid close-in combinations  
**Practical Focus:** Compact kata emphasizing body unity and efficient redirection. A bridge between external technique and internal understanding.

---

## 17. Shimpatan (新破坦 / しんぱたん, “Great New Breakthrough”)

**Level:** Rokudan–Nanadan (senior master)  
**Origin:** Toyama’s most advanced kata  
**Stances:** Variable; dynamic multi-level transitions  
**Blocks:** Combined open-hand, soft deflections  
**Strikes:** All classical techniques integrated  
**Practical Focus:** Represents mastery of adaptability and breath-movement unison. Symbolizes spiritual maturity and return to simplicity.

---

## 18. Aoi Nagi (青波 / あおいなぎ, “Blue Wave”)

**Level:** Senior master (7th dan and above)  
**Origin:** Toyama’s final conceptual kata  
**Stances:** Soft transitional  
**Blocks:** Ryūsui no uke (流水の受け / りゅうすいのうけ, flowing-water block)  
**Strikes:** Gentle redirections blending offense and defense  
**Practical Focus:** Meditative kata representing unity between body and nature. Expresses stillness in motion—the ultimate stage of Shudōkan practice.

---

## Origin of the kata

There is no known Japanese kata named 敬心形 (Keishin-kata) in any major Okinawan or mainland karate lineage: Shōtōkan (松濤館), Shōrin-ryū, Kobayashi, Matsubayashi, Shōrinji-ryū, Gōjū-ryū, Uechi-ryū, Ryūei-ryū, Motobu Udun-di, Matsumura Orthodox, Tou’on-ryū, Chitō-ryū, Shitō-ryū, Koryū White Crane (白鶴拳), none of these includes Keishin (敬心形), Keishin-gata, Keishin-dai.

Given the kanji 敬心, it was probably created in the USA in the 1950s.

## Kata movements

- Heisoku-dachi (閉足立ち): feet together  
    Bow rei (礼) and say onegai shimasu (お願いします).  
    Straighten and announce the kata name "Keishin"(敬心).  
    Left open hand on top of the right hand, lift up outwards, bring inwards, and lower. heikō-dachi (平行立ち): ready position.
    
- **Migi gedan shuto** (右手刀), right-hand low and open; this is a stance.  
    Hidari hikite (引き手): left hand fist on the hip.  ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Step with the left leg backwards into heikō-dachi (平行立ち): shoulder feet apart. The body is turned 90° left. Eyes look right. 
    
- Migi gedan soto shuto-uke (右下段外手刀受け), sweep right to left and bring all the way to the left shoulder.  
    Heikō-dachi (平行立ち).
    
- Migi jōdan yoko shuto (右上段横手刀) to the neck.  
    Hidari hikite (引き手).  
    heikō-dachi (平行立ち)  
    ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- From heikō-dachi (平行立ち), slide the **right foot backward.**  
    Right hand grabs opponent's collar to the right,  
    migi te de eri o tsukamu (右手で襟を掴む).
    2. Rotate 90° right into zenkutsu-dachi (前屈立ち): facing forward.  
    
    **hidari gyaku**-tsuki (左逆突き)
    
- Hidari mae-geri (左前蹴り).  
    Hidari zenkutsu-dachi (左前屈立ち).
    
- Migi gyaku-tsuki (右逆突き)
    
    Hidari zenkutsu-dachi (左前屈立ち). ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Bring your right fist on top of your hidari hikite (左引き手).  
    Migi Mae Geri (右前蹴り), and while the knee is still in the air...  
    migi tettsui-uchi (右鉄槌打ち): right horizontal hammer-fist strike;
    
    You may grab the opponent with your right hand.
    
- Hidari **gedan** oi-tsuki (左追い突き).  
    Migi zenkutsu-dachi (右前屈立ち).  
    Kiai (気合) !!!  
    ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Look left, turn left 90°  
    Hidari Shuto-dachi (手刀立ち): L-stance  
    gedan hidari shuto-uke (下段左手刀受け) downward left shuto block, right shuto on top of the belt knot.
    
- **migi nukite** (右貫手) spear-hand strike to the neck,  
    hidari hikite (引き手). Hidari Shuto-dachi (手刀立ち).  
    ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Look over the **left** shoulder, Turn 180 degrees to the **right**. Gedan migi shuto-uke (下段右手刀受け) downward right shuto block, Migi shuto-dachi (右手刀立ち)
    
- Slide into migi zenkutsu-dachi (右前屈立ち)  
    hidari **jōdan** shuto-tsuki (左上段手刀突き) high left spear thrust.
    
- migi hikite (右引き手).  
    ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Bring the right foot back together to heisoku-dachi (閉足立ち),  
    The **left** hand remains extended in shuto,  
    place the **left** hand on the **right shoulder**,  
    Look left.  
    Hidari gedan barai (左下段払い)
    
- heisoku-dachi (閉足立ち): feet together  
    migi hikite (右引き手).  
    hidari hikite (左引き手).
    
- ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Look over your right shoulder to the back. Right **hand** across the body to the **left ear**,  
    step **forward** with the left foot into  
    usoku-dachi (右足立ち), a **lean-away** stance.  
    Belt-height right shuto-uke (右手刀受け): You are leaning away from the attacker, twisted at the waist, facing backwards.

- Tsuru-ashi-dachi (鶴足立ち): right knee up crane stance  
    **nijū-uke** (二重受け): **double** block  
    - migi gedan-barai (右下段払い) right-hand low block,  
    - hidari age-uke (左上げ受け) left rising block

    open both hands,  
    rotate both hands **counterclockwise**,

- Left hand retracts to shuto over the belt.
    
- Hidari shuto-tsuki (左手刀突き).  
    migi mamori-te (右守り手): the right hand guards the heart.  
    Hidari zenkutsu-dachi (左前屈立ち).
    
- Migi shuto-tsuki (右手刀突き).  
    hidari mamori-te (左守り手): the left hand guards the heart.  
    Migi zenkutsu-dachi (右前屈立ち).  
    ❀ ❀ ❀
    
- Look **left**, over your shoulder. Turn **right** 180 degrees,  
    migi neko-ashi-dachi (右猫足立ち): right cat stance, close both fists, protecting (hugging) the torso
    
- **Hidari tsuru-ashi-dachi** (左鶴足立ち)**: right knee up crane stance**

- Bring the right foot in to Heisoku-dachi (閉足立ち): feet together.

# Shuhari (守破離)

https://youtu.be/Y66uMiInbnQ?si=17pdKTd8g-wv-fvj

[[SHU (守)]] - Obey: The stage of perfect copying and obsessive reproduction 
[[HA (破)]] - Break: The moment to question, adapt, and combine 
[[RI (離)]] - Transcend: When you and the skill become one

[[shuhari]] (守破離) is a Japanese concept describing the stages of mastery in traditional arts such as martial disciplines, tea ceremony, or calligraphy. The word is formed from three characters: 守 (shu), to protect or obey; 破 (ha), to break or detach; and 離 (ri), to leave or transcend.

In the Shu stage, the student follows the form and preserves the teachings exactly as handed down. There is no modification or interpretation, only faithful repetition. This phase builds discipline, humility, and respect for the foundation.

In the Ha stage, the practitioner begins to question and explore. Having internalized the basics, one starts to see where techniques bend and adapt. The student experiments within the structure, finding personal understanding and flexibility without abandoning the essence.

Finally, the Ri stage represents transcendence. The practitioner no longer relies on fixed forms, having absorbed their principles completely. The art becomes natural, expressed without conscious effort. What was once external discipline now flows from within, unified with the individual’s own character.

Shuhari is not only a framework for martial arts, but a reflection on learning itself. It teaches that mastery is a journey from imitation to understanding, and finally to creative freedom rooted in deep respect for tradition.


# Hakuchō 白鳥
I started training in local (Mt. Si School of Karate) Shobudo (正武道).  
  
**正 (shō)** — means “correct,” “true,” or “right.” It conveys righteousness, authenticity, and moral alignment. In martial philosophy, it implies practicing in accordance with truth and proper principles rather than ego or aggression.  
  
**武 (bu)** — means “martial” or “warrior.” The character combines the radicals 止 "to stop" and 戈 "spear", symbolizing the idea of “stopping the spear,” or controlling violence rather than causing it. This reflects the ethical foundation of martial arts — using strength to preserve peace.  
  
**道 (dō)** — means “way” or “path.” It comes from the Chinese dao, meaning both a road and a guiding principle. In Japanese budō, it refers to lifelong discipline and spiritual cultivation through practice.  
  
Together, Shōbudō 正武道 translates as “The True Martial Way” or “The Way of Correct Martial Conduct.” It emphasizes not merely combat skill but the pursuit of righteousness and inner balance through the martial path.  

  

## Hakuchō 白鳥, “White Heron” kata 型

  

The **first kata** that Master Kevin told me I need to learn is Hakuchō (白鳥, “White Heron”). 

Not being familiar with the Chinese kata at all, as White Heron is considered an esoteric teaching in Shotokan, I trained when I was young, and it is challenging for me to memorize them. 

  

The soft, flowing Hakucho is a good start for me, given my prior Shotokan training. My body and moves are taut, as if every move were a 気合 ("kiai") moment. In Shotokan, in every position, we were punched and kicked to make sure our bodies were tense.

  

When I researched online, I could not find anything exactly matching our school.

  

**The Hakuchō 白鳥 kata 型** is named after the “white bird,” or, in modern Japanese, literally the swan, though historically associated with the heron or crane. The imagery reflects the same grace, balance, and vigilance found in White Crane (白鶴, Hakutsuru) traditions from Fujian, China. Haku (白) means “white.” It is one of several "crane" forms found in various Okinawan karate styles, where its techniques mimic the defensive and offensive movements of a white crane. 

  

The Hakucho kata is a valuable form for understanding the historical connection between Chinese White Crane Kung Fu and Okinawan karate.

### Variations and Lineages

  
The name “Hakuchō” is often used interchangeably with other terms meaning “White Crane” across different styles, such as Hakutsuru (白鶴), Hakkaku (八鶴, “Eight Cranes”), and Paihō (白鶴, from Mandarin _pái hè_): 

- **Hakutsuru 白鶴**, 
- **c 八鶴** meaning Eight Cranes in some Okinawan Shitō-ryū and Ryūei-ryū and southern Chinese lineages
- **Paihō 白鶴**, in Okinawan, "pái hè" in Mandarin, meaning "White Crane" found in Ryuei-ryu.

Okinawan Karate Styles

- **Shito-ryu:** Kenwa Mabuni, the founder of Shito-ryu, learned several crane-based exercises and kata from Go Kenki, including versions of **Hakucho**, **Papuren**, and **c**. The Shito-ryu version of Hakucho is sometimes considered a fragment of a larger original form.
- **Goju-ryu:** In some Goju-ryu lineages, "Hakutsuru" (their common term for the crane form) is considered a "**secret**" or advanced kata, typically taught only to senior students.
- **Matsumura Shorin-ryu:** This style has its own unique and intricate Matsumura No **Hakutsuru** kata, also considered a **secret** form within the style.
- **Shotokan:** While not explicitly named Hakucho, the kata **Gankaku** ("Crane on a Rock") is the Shotokan version of the older Okinawan Chinto kata, and also incorporates crane-like stances and techniques. ![](https://img1.blogblog.com/img/video_object.png)

Key Characteristics

Animal Imitation  
The movements in the Hakucho kata are based on a crane's actions, such as stepping over or through obstacles, and striking with the fingertips mimicking a bird's beak.  
  

Balance and Fluidity  
The kata emphasizes balance, and practitioners often train in challenging environments, such as back yards, graveyards, and sandy beaches, to improve focus and stability. It is known for its graceful, fluid, yet powerful movements.  
  

Origins  
The various crane kata in Okinawan karate, including Hakucho, can largely be traced back to the teachings of Go Kenki (or Wu Xian Hui), a Chinese tea merchant and white crane kung fu master who lived on Okinawa in the early 20th century.

I came across Ruri Ohama mentioning a book by Takafumi Horie and Yoichi Ochiai titled: “Job Atlas for 10 Years From Now. How Will You Live in a World That Is About to Begin?”

I do not have access to the book, nor the language skills to read it, but Ruri did an excellent job expanding on the topic. References are listed below. I have written on this subject before, yet I must admit that my earlier understanding lacked the depth it deserved.

Artificial intelligence is accelerating at a pace that is stripping away the illusions of stability that defined the twentieth century. Professions, degrees, job titles, hierarchies, and even the mythology of a “career” are dissolving.

The argument is simple: the highest-paid roles are replaced first because they are the most expensive. CEOs, VPs, directors, senior software engineers, lawyers, doctors, consultants. After that, low-paying jobs fall by the wayside as automation becomes cheaper than labor.

I do not want to spin this into another philosophical thread. I want to prepare.

Writers like Horie and Ochiai argue that the only viable posture in a period of rapid technological acceleration is fluidity. Not flexibility in the casual sense of “being open-minded,” but a deeper quality and virtue. A mind in permanent rehearsal. A mind that trains.

The name of my blog is “Uki’s Ikigai” (宇気の生き甲斐), which can be read as my “universal spirit/energy’s purpose of living,” and documents my many interests spanning AI and machine learning, Cultural Anthropology of East Asia, philosophy, calligraphy, and other pursuits. One of my favorite quotes by Robert A. Heinlein hints that “specialization is for insects,” and I believe his words will prove prophetic. I want to use everything I know to prepare, and I think this preparation requires the mindset of a warrior, with the war being cerebral.

In past articles, I have written about habits and methods of continuous improvement, or kaizen (改善) in Japanese. There is no contradiction here. Mindset, habits, daily practice, and a wide field of knowledge to draw from are tools of the trade as much as the brushes of the shodō (書道) calligraphy master or sharp chisels and kana (鉋) planes in the toolbox of the traditional temple architect.

AI has made intelligence abundant, but it has not made mastery obsolete. It has raised the requirements for mastery. You do not compete with AI by trying to outperform it. You train yourself to become an effective user of it, a designer of workflows, a strategist, a builder of systems. This is the “machine-human interface”.

Mastery in the age of AI is not a poetic metaphor. It is a discipline. Most people will drown in abundance. The trained mind will not.

This interface is a craft. Not unlike the romantic craft of tea rooms and scrolls, the practical craft of AI tools and habit patterns, and holding attention steady under acceleration. Continuous learning, in this context, is not self-help. It is the maintenance of the instruments.

I study new ideas from books and scientific papers to refine my ability to see wider patterns. I write code to automate what I already understand. Kaizen (改善) is not a slogan. It is the refusal to stagnate.

Stability is no longer a feature of the environment. It is a convenience people assume will return. It will not. The only reliable asset now is a trained response. Adaptation is not improvisation; it is the result of accumulated practice applied under pressure.

Breadth and depth are not competing virtues. Breadth gives you more available models; depth gives you leverage when one model fails. Both are required. Between them sits ikigai, not as a romantic ideal but as a daily directive: pick a direction and work.

Modern language models compress decades of learning into seconds. Their value is not in answers but in the pressure they apply. They expose weak reasoning immediately. They punish vague questions. They demand clarity. The tools I build around them — filters, analyzers, optimizers — are not productivity tricks. They are the fundamentals of working in an environment where the volume of information will continue to dwarf human processing capacity.

The next decade will reward cognitive stability far more than raw intelligence. When information is cheap, attention becomes the scarce resource. Panic leads to dependency on systems you do not control. Training produces independence. This is why I maintain simple physical and cognitive practices: not for tradition, but because they enforce clarity without noise. Walking meditation, shodō (書道) strokes, kata (形) lines, and tea are not rituals. They are my calibration.

The world used to shift in industrial cycles. Now it shifts in model releases. Entire abstractions become obsolete in a week. Domains collapse or merge without warning. Philosophy offers posture but not protection. Preparedness is the only defensible position.

Humans will not be replaced, but untrained humans will be sidelined. The ones who remain relevant will operate with the discipline of technicians, not spectators. They will understand models, workflows, failure modes, and limits. They will know how to direct intelligence rather than drown in it.

My own compass is simple: improve one thing each day that increases my agency tomorrow. This keeps me in motion and keeps the system honest. Engineering discipline, cultural insight, physical practice, and deliberate attention intersect in that single act.

Continuous learning is not an ambition.

It is maintenance.

It is posture.

It is the only stable stance in an unstable environment.

# Ichigyō zammai (一行三昧)

---

Ichigyō zammai, written as 一行三昧, is a pattern visible across many Japanese arts.
The phrase began in Buddhist practice, where it described complete absorption in a single line of scripture. Over time, it became a wider cultural pattern that shaped craft, ceremony, martial arts, and even daily routines. 

The idea is simple: it asks for complete attention to one act, and its presence in so many Japanese traditions shows how deeply it combines discipline and beauty. 

In woodcraft, the spirit of the concept is evident in how an artisan handles tools and materials. Consider the intricacy of a complex joint with no nails or glue, which is treated as a complete world while it is being done. There is no rush (急がず, isogazu) toward the next step. Each action begins only when the previous one has settled (間, ma). Workshops feel calm because the work is paced by steady attention rather than by urgency. This rhythm gives the finished object its quiet precision.

The tea ceremony expresses the same ideal through ritual. Every motion has a beginning and an end, and the host moves through them with a mind anchored in the present. The placement of the tea bowl, the handling of the ladle, and the final act of offering tea to a guest are carried out without division of thought. This unbroken attention makes the room feel still, and it allows everyone involved to enter a shared space of respect. 

Ikebana shows how the idea extends into the aesthetics of nature. The arranger handles one stem at a time, sensing how each choice affects balance and movement. The work does not depend on the number of flowers but on the clarity with which each element is placed. The result carries a sense of harmony because it reflects a mind that has not been pulled in multiple directions. 

Martial arts carry this principle into motion. Repeating the action precisely leads to "muscle memory" and eventually to "no mind"  (無心, mushin) execution.

Every day of life reflects the same pattern. Cooking, cleaning, and studying become more grounded when attention is given to what is directly in front of the hands. Simple routines gain clarity. Even conversation becomes more meaningful when listening is not divided. The culture carries a subtle belief that one action, done wholeheartedly, builds a more composed life. 

Ichigyō zammai is not a heroic discipline. It does not require special training or dramatic commitment. It appears in ordinary acts across many domains, shaping them into something attentive and balanced. Through repeated presence in craft, ceremony, martial practice, and work, it becomes a quiet foundation for how Japanese culture approaches effort. The idea encourages a life in which a single moment, lived clearly, is enough to guide the next.

# shioyaki

Published: Tue, 02 Dec 2025 02:55:00 +0000

Original: https://ukidlucas.blogspot.com/2025/12/shioyaki.html

---

Japanese salt-grilled pompano, shioyaki, is one of those dishes where the method is so simple that the character of the fish becomes the center of the meal. 

Start by rinsing the pompano and drying it carefully, then make a couple of gentle diagonal cuts across each side. These shallow openings help the heat enter evenly and let the salt season the flesh. Coat the fish inside and out with coarse salt and let it rest for twenty to thirty minutes. This short waiting time pulls a bit of moisture to the surface, firms the flesh, and gives you that clean, focused flavor that makes shioyaki so satisfying. Before it goes into the oven, pat the fish dry again so the skin can crisp.

Heat the oven to a high temperature, about four hundred fifty degrees Fahrenheit, and place the fish on a lightly oiled rack or tray. At this heat the pompano cooks quickly, usually in fifteen to twenty minutes, and the transformation is obvious when the fins darken slightly and the skin becomes crisp while the interior stays tender. Some people turn the fish halfway through, some leave it untouched, and both are fine. The key is to keep it simple.

When the fish comes out of the oven, serve it immediately with a squeeze of lemon or a spoonful of grated daikon touched with soy sauce. Nothing heavy is needed, because the whole point of shioyaki is the clarity of the fish itself. This style rewards attention to small steps, a little patience, and the quiet satisfaction of good, honest cooking.

# awari wa hajimari 終わりは始まり

---

The phrase awari wa hajimari (終わりは始まり), "the end is the beginning," has become one of the core concepts in my life. For most, what are the undesirable chores or routines I see are actually small cycles of completion and renewal. 
I know at this point it is a widely popularized cliche, but the Marine Corps has taught me to make a perfect bed each morning. From the Japanese perspective, making the bed —or putting away a bed roll if you sleep on tatami —is not merely the end of the sleep cycle but the first meditative gesture towards the new day. Similarly, routines like a morning shower and shave make for a better day.
When I cook, I have a habit of opening jars of spices one by one, inhaling each aroma before deciding what belongs in the pot. I put the spice on the palm of my hand; do not use a spoon or a measuring cup. I never buy pre-mixed blends, because each spice has its own character and spirit. This small ritual is a moment of ichigo ichie (一期一会) — “one time, one meeting.” 

It is a conversation between nature and my senses, a way of meeting the present moment through something as ordinary as soup. I have come to understand that mindfulness does not require meditation cushions or temples; it happens in the kitchen, in the steam rising from a pot.

In Okinawa, where I once lived, this way of being seemed to exist naturally. The people there often said nankuru naisa (なんくるないさ) — “things will work out if you live sincerely.” It was not laziness or avoidance, but trust in the natural order of life. They treated their surroundings with quiet respect, believing that kami (神), the spirits of Shinto, reside in all things — the house, the tools, the garden, the wind. I feel this too. When I handle my kitchen knife or clean the counter, I sense I am tending to more than objects; I am honoring the spirit of the place. The home becomes a living jinja (神社), a personal shrine of gratitude.

I have also found this awareness in my work with tools, both physical and digital. I love Japanese joinery, not only for its precision but for the respect it shows toward wood and craftsmanship. The carpenter’s philosophy, shokunin kishitsu (職人気質), resonates with me as deeply as any teaching of Zen. To sharpen a chisel before beginning work is not preparation; it is part of the work itself. In the same way, I see software architecture — the structure before the code — as the sharpening of my craft. A well-tuned tool or a well-structured program allows the spirit of the task to flow without resistance. In both, mastery is less about performance and more about readiness.

These ideas have merged for me into a kind of personal dō (道) — a way of life. Whether I am practicing karate, writing code, or tending to a small bonsai, I feel the same rhythm. Each motion is shugyō (修行), daily discipline that refines not only skill but character. Each action ends and begins at once. The repetition is not dull; it is liberating. There is no “done,” only continuous practice.

Over time, awari wa hajimari has changed how I see everything. The end of the day, the last spoon washed, the final line of code written — none of these are true endings. They are bows, gestures of gratitude before the next beginning. This awareness brings a calm satisfaction, a kind of chōwa (調和), harmony between what I do and who I am. Life becomes less about achieving and more about participating in its cycles — the quiet flow of beginnings, endings, and the spaces between.

Mastery in the age of AI is not a poetic metaphor. It is a discipline. Most people will drown in abundance. The trained mind will not.

This interface is a craft. Not unlike the romantic craft of tea rooms and scrolls, the practical craft of AI tools and habit patterns, and holding attention steady under acceleration. Continuous learning, in this context, is not self-help. It is the maintenance of the instruments.

I study new ideas from books and scientific papers to refine my ability to see wider patterns. I write code to automate what I already understand. Kaizen (改善) is not a slogan. It is the refusal to stagnate.

Stability is no longer a feature of the environment. It is a convenience people assume will return. It will not. The only reliable asset now is a trained response. Adaptation is not improvisation; it is the result of accumulated practice applied under pressure.

This is why I maintain simple physical and cognitive practices: not for tradition, but because they enforce clarity without noise. Walking meditation, shodō (書道) strokes, kata (形) lines, and tea are not rituals. They are my calibration.

My own compass is simple: improve one thing each day that increases my agency tomorrow. This keeps me in motion and keeps the system honest. Engineering discipline, cultural insight, physical practice, and deliberate attention intersect in that single act.

Continuous learning is not an ambition.

It is maintenance.

It is posture.

It is the only stable stance in an unstable environment.

Finding a Successor to My 30-Year-Old Okinawan Gi

For more than thirty years I have trained in a heavy-duty gi I brought home from Okinawa. The label reads “Budo OMA Brand,” a name that has all but disappeared today, yet the uniform itself has survived decades of classes, sweat, and washing. It finally began to fray at the collar and crotch, and I realized that if I want to preserve it as a memory, I need to retire it before it falls apart completely.

Finding a proper replacement proved more difficult than I expected. Many modern gis are lighter, made for competition, or designed for convenience rather than longevity. I wanted something that matched the old Okinawan spirit: thick cotton, reinforced stitching, and durability measured in decades, not months.

I looked at several options. Tokaido and Shureido remain the gold standard for traditional karate craftsmanship, with precise tailoring and high-end materials. Their heavyweight models are excellent, but they also come with premium pricing. Other brands, like Wacoku, offer solid heavy cotton gis suitable for hard training, though with more variation in quality.

In the end, I found a good balance. The Ronin Brand Super Heavyweight 16oz Gi stood out as a true heavyweight uniform with the old-school feel I prefer. It has the thickness, structure, and durability I was looking for without drifting into boutique pricing. This should be a worthy successor to my old OMA Brand heirloom while I preserve the original as a reminder of where my training began.

 "Tamata and the Alliance" makes you think about the Pacific and its islands.

My visit to Guam was brief, just a long lay over on the flight from Hawaii to Okinawa. I was on the military flight in the enormous Air Force C-5 Galaxy cargo plane, I don't remember anymore if Guam was a scheduled stop, or not, but I remember vividly that our plane broke down and we had an emergency landing and evacuation on some back-strip of the Andersen Air Force base in the middle of the night amongst tall cane, or grasses. The evacuation was brief, we were not to take any personal things with us, we stood some few hundred feet away from the plane, and I just remember seeing some kind of hydraulic fluid shooting up in the fountain from the port wing. To give you some idea about what it means to realize that your plane broke over the Pacific you have to imagine that the distance from Hawaii to Guam is twice as far as California to Florida and there are only couple of small islands between: Wake Island and Marshal Islands far to the south and absolutely nothing between, except sharks. Okinawa is another New York to Denver distance away.
Next day, I needed to find some other arrangement to fly to Okinawa and that would not happen for the next 16 hours, so I hitchhiked to the beach.

I remember walking the sandy groves of the coconut palms and watching the absolutely cute brown skinned local kids playing between the palms and on the white sandy beach. Absolute paradise.

Japanese wooden boxes have a quiet elegance that stays with me. A simple hako (箱) is more than a container. It becomes part of a person’s rhythm, holding the tools, papers, or small treasures that shape daily life. When I place a box on a shelf or behind a screen, it feels like setting down a piece of intention. The room gains a sense of order, not rigid or forced, but calm.

The beauty starts with wood itself. A lightweight kiri kibako (桐木箱) feels almost unreal in the hand, as if air and grain were mixed together. The surface carries a natural script that changes with the light. Opening the lid is like opening a quiet thought. Nothing is loud, nothing demands attention, yet the presence is unmistakable. The character hako (箱) might look simple, but every box expresses a different personality.

This subtlety comes from the craft. Corners joined with kigumi (木組み) feel effortless. In reality they rest on generations of skill, an unbroken conversation between carpenter and material. The tradition of sashimono (指物) shows itself not through decoration but through restraint. You can feel it when sliding a lid, when lifting a panel, when the wood fits into place without hesitation. It is the confidence of a maker who understands that strength does not need to announce itself.

Some boxes hold shodōgu (書道具), the tools for writing. A small suzuri-bako (硯箱) with its inkstone and brush can shift a person’s mood the moment it is opened. It reminds me that writing is a practice, not an action. Other boxes store dōgu (道具) for carpentry, the chisels and planes that shape wood the same way words shape thought. These boxes gain their beauty through wear: softened edges, light scratches, the faint scent of cedar or hinoki. They become portraits of the hands that used them.

When I imagine a wall lined with boxes, I do not see storage. I see a quiet archive of ikigai (生き甲斐), each box holding something essential. A calligraphy set resting between morning sessions. A woodworking tool waiting for the next cut. A small bundle of paper for future notes. Even an empty box carries promise. It is an invitation to create, to learn, to pay attention.

A box, after all, is a small universe. It has a boundary and an interior, an outside that faces the world and an inside that holds meaning. Opening and closing it becomes a rhythm of its own. Japanese boxes capture this feeling without effort. They simply exist with purpose, like a stone placed in a garden or a brush resting on a table. Quiet, steady, sincere.

This is the esthetic that I come back to again and again. The elegance of hako (箱) as a companion in daily life, a reminder that what we choose to protect reveals what we value.

# Mono no aware 物の哀れ


Mono no aware (物の哀れ)

Mono no aware (物の哀れ) refers to a gentle awareness of impermanence. The phrase can be translated as the pathos of things (a quality that evokes sadness), although the spirit and feeling behind it feels softer than the literal words suggest. It describes the quiet emotion that arises when you recognize that everything passes, blossoms fall, summers fade, and moments slip into memory. Rather than being tragic, it invites a tender appreciation for the beauty found in things precisely because they do not last.

In everyday life this feeling appears when you notice falling leaves, or flower petals, birds flying away for the winter, even sunlight on a table that will soon shift and you sense both gratitude and a small ache. It is a way of seeing the world with sensitivity and acceptance, allowing each moment to be meaningful without trying to hold it still.

The first character 物 (mono) means things or phenomena, the second の (no) links the words in a possessive or descriptive way, and 哀れ (awa-re) conveys tenderness, sorrow, and emotional resonance. Together, they point to the quiet emotional response that arises when a person perceives the fleeting nature of life.

From a cultural anthropology perspective, Mono no aware (物の哀れ) is not simply a poetic idea. It emerged from patterns of aesthetic thought that shaped Japanese literature, religion, and everyday sensibilities for more than a thousand years. Its roots lie in Heian court culture, especially in the literary world of The Tale of Genji. In that period, sensitivity to seasonal change, the fragility of human bonds, and the subtle shifts in nature became markers of refined awareness. Poetry, painting, and personal letters all relied on the ability to feel and convey this delicate emotional shade.

The idea grew out of a worldview in which permanence was never expected. Buddhism reinforced this understanding through the concept of mujō (無常) or impermanence, which taught people to observe life as a flowing stream. Shinto added a layer of animistic appreciation for spirits within natural forms, encouraging people to approach the world with respect and emotional attention. When these traditions met, they encouraged a way of living that valued the moment without attempting to control its duration.

Mono no aware (物の哀れ) can be seen as a cultural strategy for emotional resilience. Instead of resisting change, a person learns to meet it with awareness and acceptance. This sensibility influences festivals aligned with the seasons, daily rituals of greeting and parting, and the way objects are handled with care, even when they are temporary or straightforward. Cherry blossom viewing is the clearest example. People gather to admire a beauty that lasts only a few days, and the brevity itself creates the emotional depth. The appreciation is heightened precisely because the petals will soon fall.

In this sense, Mono no aware (物の哀れ) is not only an aesthetic principle. It is a way of interpreting human experience, shaped by long historical patterns, layered religious traditions, and a cultural preference for sensitivity over control. It encourages the soft strength that comes from acknowledging that every moment arrives, shines for a time, and then moves on.


# Finding Zen in Motion - The Power of Active Meditation

Dear Friend

Despite my former [[karate]] training, I’ve never been able to sit still for long in traditional [[zazen]] [[meditation]], despite the best efforts of the [[sensei]]. Sitting in one place for extended periods doesn’t resonate with me. Instead, I’ve found peace in movement, specifically through long walks in the forest.


Active meditation isn’t a new concept; it’s been practiced for centuries by various cultures.

My inspiration came from my studies in Cultural Anthropology, particularly the Monks of [[Mount Hiei]] in [[Japan]].

These [[Tendai]] monks, known as the "[[Marathon Monks]]," engage in [[kaihōgyō 回峰行]] (circling the mountain), a challenging practice that involves walking great distances over long periods (for example, 84km per day for 100 days) in rice straw sandals.



I would like to dedicate this article to them as they are amazing.

Their physical and deep spiritual journey emphasizes the connection between movement and mindfulness.
It is also related to [[Fudō Myōō (不動明王)]], the one who cuts through delusions. The Buddhist temple I attended in [[Okinawa]], [[Japan]], was dedicated to the same fierce Bodhhisatva.


Stepping into the forest, the trail ahead of me is not just a path but a gateway to another realm. As I walk, I focus on my breath, the texture, and the smell of often wet Pacific Northwest moss and ferns, which are very similar to those of mountains in Japan. Even the trails I frequent on Tiger Mountain hold a special connection for me. They remind me of the tiger in the [[Shotokan]] karate emblem and the evergreens that the founder, Master [[Gichin Funakoshi]], loved so much.

Each step is a repetitive mantra, each breath a moment of reflection.

As a [[Marine]], I also found an equivalent in the string of knots made of 550 cord, which we used, similar to [[Buddhist]] beads, a type of rosary. Much like each bead, each knot represents the distance traveled — the number of paces or prayers and serves as a point of focus, a reminder of discipline, and a tool for mindfulness.


Credit “Wardog Surplus” on Amazon

Walking daily, usually about three miles in the forest, allows me to disconnect from the chaos of daily life. It’s a time to let my thoughts flow freely and find solutions to problems.

I wouldn't be a proper Zen Buddhist if I did not mention that our all-time favorite trail is the "Poo-Poo" trail. Yes, there is time to get lofty, but there are dog shit bags on the side of the trail, too. Both can be equally enlightening, though they may not be equally enjoyable. Let me know if you want to learn how Zen monks use contrast to break mental barriers and stereotypes.

As I walk, I notice the way the sunlight filters through the leaves, especially after the rain, and enjoy the scent of cypress trees in the air.

Sometimes, I sit on the trunk of a fallen tree, where the sun comes over through the canopy. These are my happy moments of reflection.


Photo by author (Uki), Pacific Northwest

So, the next time you feel overwhelmed or need a mental reset, consider walking. Embrace the journey, not the destination. Remember, meditation is not about how you sit; it’s about how you find your center, and for some of us, that center is found in motion.


Respectfully,

Uki D. Lucas

Japanese salt-grilled pompano, shioyaki, is one of those dishes where the method is so simple that the character of the fish becomes the center of the meal. 

Start by rinsing the pompano and drying it carefully, then make a couple of gentle diagonal cuts across each side. These shallow openings help the heat enter evenly and let the salt season the flesh. Coat the fish inside and out with coarse salt and let it rest for twenty to thirty minutes. This short waiting time pulls a bit of moisture to the surface, firms the flesh, and gives you that clean, focused flavor that makes shioyaki so satisfying. Before it goes into the oven, pat the fish dry again so the skin can crisp.

Heat the oven to a high temperature, about four hundred fifty degrees Fahrenheit, and place the fish on a lightly oiled rack or tray. At this heat the pompano cooks quickly, usually in fifteen to twenty minutes, and the transformation is obvious when the fins darken slightly and the skin becomes crisp while the interior stays tender. Some people turn the fish halfway through, some leave it untouched, and both are fine. The key is to keep it simple.

When the fish comes out of the oven, serve it immediately with a squeeze of lemon or a spoonful of grated daikon touched with soy sauce. Nothing heavy is needed, because the whole point of shioyaki is the clarity of the fish itself. This style rewards attention to small steps, a little patience, and the quiet satisfaction of good, honest cooking.

Shuto-dachi (手刀立ち)

Shuto-dachi is the stance most people recognize from the open-hand blocks of karate. The feet form an L-shape, the front foot pointing forward and the back foot turned out. The weight is shared evenly, which makes it more stable than a cat stance but lighter than a deep back stance. It is used in many Shorin-based systems because it supports the natural mechanics of shuto-uke. The hips stay open, the shoulders relaxed, and the body is ready to rotate or step without losing balance. In practice, the stance teaches calmness, centered posture, and controlled movement, which is why it appears early in kata like Keishin.

Many online videos warn about vegetables you should never eat, especially those rich in oxalates. The tone is dramatic. The reality is much more benign. 

I grew up with Polish soups like barszcz (beet soup) and pomidorowa (tomato soup), and now I often cook Japanese bowls like miso shiru (味噌汁) or soba (蕎麦). My soups are based on broth, slow cooking, and simple balance. They do not fit the fear-based approach that thrives on the internet. 

The research on oxalates has a solid foundation, but it does not condemn these foods. The risk mainly applies to people who already form kidney stones or who eat high-oxalate foods without enough minerals. 

When vegetables like beets meet warm broth, the story changes. A good bone base carries calcium and magnesium. These bind oxalates in the gut, lower absorption, and make the meal gentle on the kidneys. Traditional cooking understood this long before anyone named the chemistry. 

In Poland, soups start with bones, water, and herbs. In Japan, miso and soba are made with dashi (出汁), fermented and fresh plants, seaweed, and mushrooms. A bowl of warm broth does more for health than any YouTube warning list. It keeps fluid moving through the body, supports digestion, and brings minerals that soften the sharper vegetables. 

So when I hear strict rules about what never to eat, I remind myself that a good soup supports a long life better than anxiety ever will.

# Study, teaching and practice of buddhism

I have decided to openly pursue the study, teaching, and practice of buddhism.
It is of a little secret that I have been attracted to buddhism for at least the last ten years, but there were many things I had to get right in my mind, and in my life, to come to this stage of affairs.

Buddhism was originally meant to be, and shall be, a philosophy of life, a way to the enlightement, not a religion.

There shall not be any friction between practicing of buddhism and believing in any other religion as to the its faith. The buddhism is not about believing, but about understanding, or perceiving. That understanding comes on the logical, and the intuitive (a non-dualistic), levels. The choice of the religion to follow is one's personal and should be dictated by the conciousness. There are, however, harmful faiths that conceal behind traditions and religions. One should study much and develop a broad perspective.
3) Worldly Attachments.
It is contrary to the understanding of the buddhism to maintain celibacy, to renounce the World, or any such extremes. It is essential to embrace the World as it is and as the naturalists say it "to thread lightly". It is essential to understand one's own attachments and their effects. However, it is appropriate to question and abandon the attachments that are perceived as ill-natured. Buddhism is a middle path.
4) Karma.
The core of the buddhism is a full awarness of the law of causation. One smallest event causes ten thousand things to happen. Any affects all. One shall be always aware of that karmic law. At the same time one shall not fall into traps of logical dualism.
5) Good and bad.
There are neither good nor bad actions in buddhism, there is only suchness of those actions. Any action can cause positive and negative, or both, consequent effects, yet conciously bad actions leave their karmic mark and intuitively shall be avoided. It will be a sign of a good practice when good-natured actions will intuitively replace any ill ones. 
6) Rules.
There shall be no commandmants, precepts, nor rules in buddhism. All teachings shall be taken as suggestions for one to meditate on and come to their full understanding. The follower of the buddhism shall not ignore the ancient teachings and study them in-depth, however one shall not follow mindlessly, nor literally, any of such teachings.
7) Compassion.
One shall develop a compassionate approach to the World as one cannot exist without the World. Onyone with this understanding of buddhism should propagate it thru the World in a bodhisattva fashion, such compassion shall be an agent and a messenger of the buddhism.
8) Daily practice.
The practice of the buddhism shall be one's daily life, not a mere visit to the temple, few minutes of a chant, yoga practice, sitting meditation, nor retreat in the nature. Those various practices may be integrated into daily buddhism, but if practiced in mindless vacum can be also harmful. One shall choose daily tasks to reflect their buddhist mind.
9) Training.
Mind does not exist without body and they are both same on one level. One shall train their minds as much as their bodies to achieve a radiant and healthy results. One shall follow the study, the works, the exercise and the appropriate diet.
10) Masters.
One shall take time to learn from old masters that came before, improve upon, respect their efforts and support their causes.
11) Worship.
No buddhist shall be considered a saint, nor be worshipped as such. The creations of the mind and symbols shall be taken as just such. Traditions and art shall be respected, developed, and protected for the future generations.
12) Home Temple.
All past teachings should be preserved and attempt shall be made to propagate many of them for all to come. It is a natural step for one to eventually retire from the daily life and dedicate one's time to the study, the practice, and the teaching at the home temple. Because the home temple provides a peacefull environment, more suitable for the study and the colaboration, it is recommended to support one, and eventually spend time in the home temple passing on the knowledge to the next generations.

# Usoku-dachi (右足立ち)

Published: Fri, 21 Nov 2025 06:18:03 +0000

Original: https://ukidlucas.blogspot.com/2025/11/usoku-dachi.html

---

Usoku-dachi (右足立ち)  “lean-away stance” is not a step or a foot-change. It is a torso rotation inside an existing shuto-dachi.

Starting from hidari shuto-dachi (左手刀立ち)
 (left foot forward, right foot at 90 degrees, even weight) 
The legs do not move at all.
The feet remain exactly in place, keeping the original L-shape 
The karateka rotates the upper body to the right, looking backward over the right shoulder.
During the rotation, the practitioner performs gedan shuto-uke (下段手刀受け) backward.
The stance remains essentially shuto-dachi, but now the torso is facing the opposite direction.
In short, Usoku-dachi = shuto-dachi with a backward torso rotation, no foot movement, weight unchanged.

右 (migi / u) = right
足 (ashi / soku) = foot or leg
立ち (dachi) = stance

# 宇氣法 (Uki-hō)

Created on: 2025-10-26 at 21:09


  

宇氣法 (Uki-hō) — The Method of Universal Energy

  

  

Uki-hō (宇氣法) began not as a doctrine, but as a gradual realization.

It formed somewhere between hard training and quiet observation, over many years when discipline became habit and reflection became necessity. There was no moment of creation, only the slow settling of ideas that had been living in the background of daily practice.

  

  

From Structure to Stillness

  

  

The early years of practice were defined by structure. Shotokan karate and the discipline of the Marine Corps shared a similar rhythm — direct, efficient, absolute. Progress came through repetition, and purpose was measured in precision.

Yet over time, structure revealed its limit. The precision remained, but the sense of direction blurred. It was in Okinawa that this began to soften. There, karate and kobudō existed without pretense. Movements curved where once they struck straight. Breathing mattered as much as technique. The locals trained quietly, smiled easily, and carried no need to prove what they already were.

  

  

The Island and Its Lesson

  

  

Okinawa taught without instruction. The people, the food, and the rhythm of life formed a kind of living philosophy — one that valued presence over performance. Meals were simple, shared, and unhurried. Work was steady, not rushed. Longevity seemed less a goal than a by-product of balance.

Kindness was not something practiced; it was simply how people moved through the world.

  

  

Quiet Integration

  

  

After leaving Okinawa, the lessons did not fade. They settled slowly, like ink absorbed into paper. Aikidō added circularity and acceptance. Calligraphy opened a way to translate motion into emotion. Traditional wood joinery revealed how harmony could be built, not forced. Bonsai offered patience — the kind that extends beyond a single season.

  

Through these practices, Uki-hō took shape not as a method to teach others, but as a way to live more honestly with oneself.

  

  

A Philosophy of Two Roots

  

  

At its heart, Uki-hō is a meeting of Zen and Stoicism. From Zen comes awareness of impermanence, the attention to this breath, this line, this moment. From Stoicism comes steadiness — the understanding that control lies only within one’s own choices, and peace in accepting what cannot be changed.

  

Together they form a posture of quiet strength — active without aggression, disciplined without rigidity.

  

  

The Choice of “Hō”

  

  

The use of 法 (hō) — “method,” “principle,” or “natural law” — reflects the inward nature of this path. A ryū (流) teaches a lineage; a hō observes a way things already are.

Uki-hō is not a style of movement but a recognition of energy’s flow — in body, craft, conversation, and leadership alike.

  

It asks nothing of titles, ranks, or followers. It simply describes the patient attempt to live with coherence — the same coherence one seeks in a brush stroke, a joint between timbers, or the curve of a wind-swept pine.

  

  

The Present Understanding

  

  

At fifty-two, Uki-hō is no longer a pursuit of mastery. It has become a way of moving through work and relationships, of managing with calm rather than command.

It is a daily reminder that clarity often arrives unannounced, that balance must be rebuilt every morning, and that the deepest practice may remain invisible to others — and that this is entirely fine.

  

  

Closing Thought

  

  

Uki-hō remains unfinished, as all living things are. It is not a teaching but a practice; not a system but an atmosphere.

It grows quietly wherever breath, intention, and kindness align — in the dojo, in the office, or in the forest after rain


Ryū (流) implies a school, style, or lineage — something meant to be transmitted, categorized, and perhaps even defended. It carries historical weight, but also hierarchy and the expectation of discipleship. Calling something a ryū often suggests a public declaration: this is my system.

  

Hō (法), by contrast, means method, principle, or natural order. It does not claim ownership of truth — it acknowledges discovery. Hō suggests that what you’ve learned is not invented, but found through practice. It aligns with Zen modesty and Stoic restraint: the law is greater than the person who follows it.

  

So yes — after thirty years of training, reflection, and synthesis, naming it Uki-hō feels neither self-important nor artificial. It’s not branding; it’s recognition — a quiet marker that the path has matured into coherence.

  

You’re not founding a system; you’re naming the way you walk.


# Blank slate

A blank slate in the Western sense often means an untouched mind, free from preconceptions. In Japanese culture, there is no single direct equivalent. Instead, several concepts together express the spirit of beginning from emptiness, openness, or readiness.

  

Here are the closest ideas.

  

1. Shoshin (初心)

Often translated as “beginner’s mind.”

This is the idea of approaching anything with freshness, curiosity, and without pre-formed judgment. Shunryu Suzuki described it as a state where possibilities are many because the mind is not crowded by assumptions.

It is not naïveté. It is deliberate openness.

  

2. Mushin (無心)

Literally “no-mind.”

This is the empty, unclouded state reached in budo and Zen. The mind is not blank due to ignorance, but blank because attachments, fear, and ego have dropped away.

Mushin is a space that allows precise action without hesitation.

  

3. Ma (間)

The intentional space or interval.

Ma is not emptiness as absence, but emptiness as potential. A pause in conversation, a gap in design, the open area in a room all hold meaning. This is a cultural understanding that empty space has its own presence.

  

4. Mu (無)

Simply “nothing” or “non-being,” but deeply layered.

In Zen, Mu is the state before ideas take form. It is the fertile void. It resembles a philosophical blank slate, although it carries spiritual weight rather than cognitive theory.

  

5. Hakikei or blank page in arts

In calligraphy and ink painting, the empty paper is already part of the composition. Japanese aesthetics treat the unpainted area not as blankness but as the ground from which meaning arises.

# Bukkake udon (ぶっかけうどん)
#Japan #Japanese #CulturalAnthopology 

Do you know how it is when you stumble upon a foreign phrase that's wildly different in your language? It can be quite the head-scratcher. I was watching a YouTube video recently; a girl walks into a Japanese restaurant, and the waiter offers her a special of Bukkake udon (ぶっかけうどん). He also hands her a steaming white towel to clean herself. She's humorously uncomfortable and leaves, excusing herself.

I'm no stranger to Japanese culture; I've lived there for three years and sincerely appreciate its nuances. But this one puzzled me, so I had to look it up.

[![](https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEiOMZNBvwzjP30I_iUKpkOX1ni_nCfCpR8uT39BOZktYb2QzI7_P2RcBKMrjeu3IGgswzQgw6Npcfzcdd-1-ptH8ogr-Z4ByssAixowWh4YMADRdtTu4SQmu4QlzJ_8Ri6sXs9_mWyMIcVrQlkeTwV1Uh5hp0plpxfFoscsxagn_O3rd2TqCNVGJA/w400-h300/IMG_0191.HEIC)](https://www.blogger.com/blog/post/edit/3310975/3980947898042180012#)

  

  

Bukkake udon is a legitimate Japanese noodle dish. The term bukkake, in this culinary context, simply means "to pour over." So you get cold or warm udon noodles with a flavorful sauce (like soy sauce or dashi) poured over them, topped off with green onions, grated radish, and tempura bits, and you get the idea.

  

However, step outside of Japan and _bukkake_ has a different connotation. 

---

Bukkake is a slang term in the realm of adult entertainment with an explicit sexual meaning related to the "pouring over" theme. This contrast can sometimes cause humor and confusion.

So, when you're in a Japanese restaurant and the waiter cheerfully announces "bukkake udon," don't freak out! It's just a cooking term in Japan, not an invitation to something risqué.

[Matcha](https://www.womenshealthmag.com/food/a27127259/matcha-powder-benefits/) is having a moment as the new super drink on the block. Made by grinding up young tea leaves from the [[Camellia sinensis]] plant (the same one that black and oolong come from), matcha boasts a number of benefits, from [promoting healthier cholesterol levels to improving brain function](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7796401/). If you're looking to swap out your morning coffee but still want an energy boost, you may be wondering, _Does matcha have caffeine?_

First, you should know you're doing your body a solid by switching to matcha. “It provides the same health benefits of green tea, amplified,” says nutritionist [Jessica Cording](https://jessicacordingnutrition.com/about/), RD, the author of _[The Little Book of Game-Changers](https://www.amazon.com/dp/163228068X?linkCode=ogi&tag=womenshealth-auto-20&ascsubtag=%5Bartid%7C2140.a.19921072%5Bsrc%7Cwww.google.com%5Bch%7C%5Blt%7C%5Bpid%7Cf8b42818-4ba1-4d8b-86e9-8d54eeee7c6f)_.

Since you _consume_ the actual tea leaves when drinking matcha, you get about twice the amount of the antioxidant EGCG that you would from drinking regular green tea, adds New York-based nutritionist [Asako Miyashita](https://www.asakonutritionsalon.com/), RDN.

In case you're not familiar, though originally from China, matcha has strong ties to Japan. In the 12th century, Japanese Buddhist monk Myoan Eisai left for China to find green tea, according to Miyashita. “Eisai realized that drinking matcha before meditation helped maintain his concentration," she says. So he brought the matcha back to Japan and wrote a book called _Kissa Yojoki_ (_Drinking Tea_).

Traditionally, only matcha made from Japanese tea leaves was considered legitimate, but these days, it's produced pretty much everywhere.

“The tea leaves are cultivated under cover to avoid direct sunlight for three to four weeks from April to May,” Miyashita explains. “This process increases the leaves' chlorophyll content and amino acid content, and creates a darker green color.” It also suppresses the formation of catechin, the compound that gives green tea a bitter taste.

## **Is matcha high in caffeine?**

The short answer is it depends. Since you drink the actual tea leaves with matcha, though, it contains more caffeine than your standard cup o' green.

Like other teas—and coffee!—the amount of caffeine in a cup of matcha can vary, says nutritionist [Keri Gans](https://kerigansny.com/about/), RD, author of _[The Small Change Diet](https://www.amazon.com/dp/1451608888?linkCode=ogi&tag=womenshealth-auto-20&ascsubtag=%5Bartid%7C2140.a.19921072%5Bsrc%7Cwww.google.com%5Bch%7C%5Blt%7Csale%5Bpid%7C6bbfbe58-caac-46d5-ac22-c0e683c3aab0)_. Typically, an eight-ounce cup of matcha, which is made with one teaspoon of matcha powder, contains 70 milligrams of caffeine. “However, depending on how concentrated one likes their cup, it could have a little less or more,” she says.

## **But wait...Is matcha stronger than coffee?**

In general, a cup of matcha will contain less caffeine than [a cup of coffee](https://www.womenshealthmag.com/food/a29033809/healthy-coffee/). • Target: usually ribs, jaw, or under the arm. 

• Power: comes from speed, not penetration. 

• Impact: quick, sharp snap with the edge of the foot. 

• Body use: minimal hip rotation, the leg accelerates and recoils immediately. 

 

Purpose: speed, surprise, and creating an opening. 

 

 

Yoko geri (横蹴り) 

 

 

Yoko geri means side kick (general term). 

It comes in two classic forms: 

 
Yoko geri keage – snapping side kick (rare, same family as above). 
Yoko geri kekomi (蹴込み) – thrusting side kick. 

 

 

When people say yoko geri they almost always mean kekomi, the thrusting one. 

 

 

Yoko geri kekomi 

 

 

• Motion: driving thrust straight into the opponent. 

• Target: ribs, solar plexus, hip, knee. 

• Power: hip rotation and full body weight. 

• Impact: heel pushes through the target. 

• Body use: chamber, extend, and push through. 

 

Purpose: stopping power, breaking structure, creating distance.
