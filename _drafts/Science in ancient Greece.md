 Science in ancient Greece was a complex and diverse area of study that laid the foundation for many of our [[modern scientific disciplines]]. The ancient Greeks made significant contributions in a variety of fields, including physics, biology, astronomy, mathematics, and medicine.

Here are some key highlights:

1.  **[[physics]]**: The ancient Greeks made significant contributions to the field of physics. For example, the philosopher [[Parmenides]] is known for his argument that [[reality]] is unchanging and that all change is an illusion. Meanwhile, his pupil [[Zeno of Elea]] proposed several [[paradox]]es that have spurred philosophical and scientific discussions about the nature of [[time]] and [[space]].
    
2.  **[[Biology]]**: The Greek philosopher [[Aristotle]] made significant contributions to the field of biology. He performed extensive observations of [[natural phenomena]] and classified animals into different species. His texts, such as "[[History of Animals]]," "[[Generation of Animals]]," and "[[Parts of Animals]]," were influential for centuries after his death.
    
3.  **[[astronomy 1]]**: The Greeks also made major advancements in astronomy. [[Aristarchus of Samos]] proposed a [[heliocentric model]] of the [[solar system]], although his views were not widely accepted until much later. [[Hipparchus of Nicea]] is known for his work in establishing the basics of [[Trigonometry 1]] and for developing a reliable method for predicting [[solar eclipses]]. Furthermore, [[Ptolemy]]'s [[geocentric model]] of the universe, outlined in his work "[[The Almagest]]," was the prevailing c[[osmological system]] in the [[Western]] and [[Arab]] worlds until it was superseded by the [[heliocentric model]] during the [[Scientific Revolution]].
    
4.  **[[Mathematics in Ancient Greece 1]]**: Ancient Greece was a powerhouse in the field of mathematics. [[Euclid]]'s "[[Elements]]" is one of the most important works in the history of mathematics, laying out the foundational [[axioms of geometry]]. [[Pythagoras]] is best known for the [[Pythagorean theorem]], a fundamental principle in geometry. [[Archimedes of Syracus]]e made significant contributions to the understanding of [[levers]], the principle of [[buoyancy]], and invented an early form of [[calculus 1]].
    
5.  **[[medicine 1]]**: The field of medicine also saw significant advancements during the time of ancient Greece. The physician [[Hippocrates]], often called the "[[Father of Medicine]]," established a medical school on the [[island of Kos]] and is credited with being the first person to believe that diseases were caused naturally, not because of superstition or gods. He introduced the [[Hippocratic Oath]], a code of [[ethical conduct]] for doctors, which is still in use today.
    

The scientific methods and principles developed by the ancient Greeks have had a profound impact on the development of science and continue to influence our understanding of the world.
