# Your career roadmap.



![img](https://media-exp1.licdn.com/dms/image/C5612AQGMj4EgiIeUmw/article-cover_image-shrink_423_752/0?e=1600300800&v=beta&t=w3ZieG_vlvjctKqlJBPvgp06PQvlytD39VF6l3NDGqY)

## Manage the Distractions

In today's world, distractions are abound. 

Reader beware! As you are reading it, I am distracting you from what you really should be doing.

Every day, there are hundreds of new social media posts, new great ideas, and gadgets, which, we feel, we should become familiar with. 

There is a whole advisory industry, especially on YouTube, making millions by telling us what to think and what to buy.

You could refuse to deal with it and close the doors, many have deleted their Facebook, or Instagram. In fact if you do so, there are many retro-niche markets out there you could be doing. 

Or, you can try to manage distractions wisely. 

Personally, I love learning new ideas, if I had an unlimited amount of time I would study more things, but that is not the reality and I do have to make selections. Please read on, I have some techniques to follow below.



## We all are facing dilemmas

In addition to tiny distractions, here are few dilemmas I am constantly facing:

- Should I invest the time and effort and get an advanced degree, or at least a new certification?
- Should I be studying the machine learning, the autonomous driving, or the robotics?
- Should I spend time to write, or update my own apps?
- Should I work on weekends with friends on a new start-up?

Trying to do everything will surely lead to a failure and depression, so I have to be very diligent at prioritizing.

**Make a list, make some hard choices.**



## Eliminate bad choices



It is important to eliminate things you do not want to focus on:

- **"Commodity jobs.**" These days everyone, and their grandma, can do IT. Yes, pun intended, so **please focus and be original.**
- "**Nay sayers**" - Nothing will bring you down as a "friend" who is a psychopathic pessimist, most of the people are. **Do not talk to them**.
- "**Complainers**" - Again, people with victim syndrome will justify about anything in their life, then spend a rest of the day on Facebook. **Do not talk to them**.
- "**False authority**" - Surprise, your bosses, parents, what-have-you, could be making some terrible career decisions. Ask me how do I know. "[One word: plastics](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PSxihhBzCjk)" might have been a good advice in sixties, sillicon in fifties, [Pets.com in 2000](https://money.howstuffworks.com/10-biggest-ipo-flops4.htm), and "There is an app for this" in 2010, but today, maybe you should follow [Mike Rowe](https://www.amazon.com/kindle-dbs/entity/author/B07QN9H6QF?_encoding=UTF8&node=1240885011&offset=0&pageSize=12&searchAlias=stripbooks&sort=author-sidecar-rank&page=1&langFilter=default#formatSelectorHeader).
- I cannot list all the bad things you have to eliminate, they will be specific to your situation, but I suggest you list then in your little red book.



## Choose what is right for you

So, you'd ask yourself the question, what is the next, next thing? What should I focus on in the next few months?

Review your interests, talents, experience, and build upon it.

If you have nothing goinf on yet, follow what interests you because otherwise, you probably won't stay on course. 

Do not jump around, select one single thing you want to do in 2 years. Life is longer than you think, you can change your mind 20 times in your career.

**Once the decision about your future is made, you can relax and study.**



## Pivots

I do make this kind of career direction choices every-so-often, then I micro-pivot by a few degrees. I believe we should always build upon our prior experiences. Starting from scratch is generally a terrible idea.

Here are some of my own [pivots](https://www.amazon.com/Pivot-Jenny-Blake-audiobook/dp/B01KIPGQNG/ref=sr_1_14?dchild=1&keywords=the+next%2C+next+thing&qid=1594860440&s=audible&sr=1-14):

- in 1997 I decided to leave USMC and study computers
- in 1998 I changed from Electronics/Automotive Mechanics to Web IT
- in 2001 I changed to Java Web programming
- in 2009 I changed to Android Java Mobile programming
- in 2016 I **started to study machine learning** programming for automotive
- in 2017 I started to work for Harman on Android Infotament Systems
- in 2019 I started to work in ADAS, Autonomous Driving



## Checkpoints to follow



Here is the path I have developed and follow in my career, you can use then as checkpoints:

- Pick something you want to do about 2 years from now, 
- it has to be meaningful to you
- it has to be useful -- people must be willing to pay for it.
- Study it. Get basic understanding of what you are doing. Beginnings are slow and hard.
- Study a lot, at some point you will see a dramatic increase in your understanding,
- look up "[David Goggins](https://www.amazon.com/Cant-Hurt-Me-David-Goggins-audiobook/dp/B07KKP62FW/ref=sr_1_1?dchild=1&keywords=David+Goggins&qid=1594846710&s=audible&sr=1-1)" if you wonder how much you should practice.
- Listen to [Malcolm Gladwell’s book "Outliers"](https://www.amazon.com/Outliers-Malcolm-Gladwell-audiobook/dp/B001LNK9C4/ref=sr_1_5?dchild=1&keywords=Malcolm+Gladwell&qid=1594846569&s=audible&sr=1-5) to understand how much you need to study.
- Read everything written on the subject. Carry a few "want to read" articles (eBooks) with you at all times. There is [more articles published](https://www.paperswithcode.com/search?q_meta=&q=machine+learning) then anyone can consume. I have a Kindle with over 900 scientific papers on it, most unread.
- Become a "go-to expert", 
- **teach** at meetups, organize a special interest group,
- **Write** articles, or blog post, they do not have to be very good you are doing it for yourself.
- Write books, it is a tall order, I know.
- Make turorials (e.g. videos on YouTube).
- Get references for prior-jobs well done.
- Review job requirements, update LinkedIn profile accordingly.
- Find professional connections on LinkedIn, more is better, I have about 8,000.
- Apply for the jobs.
- Prepare for the interviews.
- Adjust your path and pivot every two years.

## 15-minute rule

The great opportunities come to people who are highly trained and ready. There is a saying "When the student is ready, the teacher appears." 

You have to be mindful to **remind yourself every 15 minutes** to make a decision on "**what to do now" to become a better human being**. 

This is one of my critical principles, this 15-minute evaluation of the time well spent. Sometimes the answer will be to eat, or sleep, or laugh with friends, sometimes it will be to get out to breathe and exercise. 

The differentiator, however, will be **how often you will decide to** study and work hard on your goals. It is all about [good habits](https://www.amazon.com/The-Power-of-Habit-Charles-Duhigg-audiobook/dp/B007EJSMC8/ref=sr_1_3?crid=3U89LRGO59DID&dchild=1&keywords=power+of+habit&qid=1594853861&sprefix=power+of+hab%2Caps%2C171&sr=8-3). Once again, in case you have missed it, your **habbits will define your sucess**. 

Suprisingly, a simple statement works for me very well, "I shall not be a lazy person." That is all that is needed, I pick up and get going.



## Back to Basics

The next aspect of the career development is **keeping up to date with the basics**, in my case, the computer science fundamentals.

The next section talks about computer science, but it applies to anything, really. The military professionals will tell you "Smooth is fast and fast is smooth".

Honestly, it does not matter how hard you work on daily basis, you will not pass the "whiteboard hazing", err "the interview", without any preparation. I lost countless of interviews with fine companies like Amazon, Google and Facebook to what I've felt was a "power trip" of some engineer without any social skills in a cookie factory. For years I was saying, "Why do I need that? I make good money here and now". 

Only later, I have read books and articles on interviewing and realized that the "whiteboard" is simply a thing the computer companies do and that **people prepare by studying** algorithms and zen-koan-like **mind-bender problems for months**. This is some great news for idle students, but not so great for busy people who have real jobs (and families). This is a particularly painful for managers like me who deal with projects and teams and do not write code for a living. Still, **to get a job in a top company you have to have outstanding skills**.

Thankfully, there is a huge backlash, and HR departments are realizing that ability to "[invert a binary tree](https://twitter.com/mxcl/status/608682016205344768?lang=en)" on a whiteboard has no correlation with creating a beautiful, or useful products. The focus in next few years should be on **"thinking on your feet" and diversity of experience**.

My suggestion for anyone, would be to have a **profession-oriented blog** and for any software engineer a [GitHub account](https://github.com/UkiDLucas), and to **be prolific on daily basis**.



## Keep the chin up

Finally, if you are interviewing, do not get discouraged by numerous rejections. 

In fact, you do not want a job that does not fit you. 

Eventually, if you really apply yourself, and if you are a half-decent human being, **you will find a great team that will love you, while willing to pay well for the privilege**.

This post should not be considered a "shortcut", but rather, a best practice on how to organize your career raodmap and prioritize your options.
