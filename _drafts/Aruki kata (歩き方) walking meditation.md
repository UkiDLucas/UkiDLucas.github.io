 **Finding Zen in Motion: The Power of Active Meditation**

---

Despite my former karate training, I’ve never been able to sit still for long in traditional za-zen meditation.  The concept of sitting in one place for extended periods just doesn’t resonate with me. Instead, I’ve found my peace in movement, specifically through long walks in the forest. 

Active meditation isn’t a new concept; it’s been practiced for centuries by various cultures. 

My inspiration came from my studies in Cultural Anthropology, particularly the Monks of Mount Hiei in Japan. 

These Tendai monks, known as the "Marathon Monks," engage in kaihōgyō 回峰行 (circling the mountain), a challenging practice that involves walking great distances over long periods (for example 84km per day for 100 days). Their journey is not just physical but deeply spiritual, emphasizing the connection between movement and mindfulness. It is also related to Fudō Myōō (不動明王) to which the temple I attended in Okinawa was dedicated.

I would love to write a whole article about them, if you are interested, as they are amazing.

Stepping into the forest, the trail ahead of me is not just a path but a gateway to a deeper state of awareness. As I walk, I focus on my breath, the texture and the smell of often wet Pacific Northwest moss and ferns, in fact very similar to that of mountains in Japan. Even the trails I frequent on Tiger Mountain hold a special connection for me. They remind me of the tiger in the Shotokan karate emblem and the evergreens that Master Gichin Funakoshi loved so much.

Each step is a mantra, each breath a moment of reflection. 

As a former Marine, I also find a parallel in the string of knots (550 cord) we used, which is similar to Buddhist beads (type of rosary). Each knot, much like each bead, is the distance traveled, number of paces, serves as a point of focus, a reminder of discipline, and a tool for mindfulness.

Walking, usually, three miles in the forest allows me to disconnect from the chaos of daily life. It’s a time to let my thoughts flow freely, to find solutions to problems. 

I wouldn't be a proper Zen Buddhist, if I did not mention that the favorite trail is named "Poo-poo" trail. Yes, there is time to get lofty, but there are dog shit bags on the side of the trail, too. Both are equally enlightening.

I notice the way the sunlight filters through the leaves, especially after the rain, the songs of birds, and the scent of cypress tree in the air. These sensory experiences ground me, helping me to stay anchored in the present.
Sometimes I sit on the trunk of the fallen tree, where the sun comes over thru the canopy. These are my happy moments. Moments of reflection.

So, the next time you feel overwhelmed or in need of a mental reset, consider taking a walk. Embrace the journey, not the destination, find your rhythm, and let the world around you guide you to a place of peace and tranquility. Remember, meditation is not about how you sit; it’s about how you find your center, and for some of us, that center is found in motion.
